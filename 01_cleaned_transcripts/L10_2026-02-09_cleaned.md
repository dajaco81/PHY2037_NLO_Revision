| χ⁽²⁾ | ~2×10⁻¹² | m/V |

These are tiny — which is why you don't see SHG when sunlight hits a window (the field is far too weak).

## Dispersion

Recall from first-year optics: in materials, different wavelengths of light travel at different speeds because they experience different refractive indices. This is **dispersion**.

$$n(\omega) = \text{function of frequency}$$

For **normal dispersion** (the usual case in lossless transparent materials): n increases with ω (higher frequency → higher refractive index). This is why a prism splits white light into a rainbow.

> *"The famous Pink Floyd album cover: the prism splitting white light. Blue bends more than red because it experiences a higher refractive index."*

The wave vector k is related to refractive index:

$$k = \frac{n\omega}{c}$$

## Why Phase Matching Matters

Consider sum frequency generation (SFG): two photons at ω₁ and ω₂ interact in a material to generate ω₃ = ω₁ + ω₂.

Inside the material, atoms at every point z are oscillating and emitting tiny dipole fields. For the total output signal to build up, all these individual contributions must arrive at the detector **in phase** (constructive interference).

**Phase matching condition:**

> **[RED BOX]**

$$k_1 + k_2 = k_3$$

Or equivalently, the phase mismatch:

$$\Delta k = k_3 - k_1 - k_2 = 0$$

**If Δk = 0:** Every atom along the propagation direction z contributes constructively → signal builds up linearly with z → intensity scales with **N²** (where N is the number of atoms).

**If Δk ≠ 0:** The signal oscillates with z — some regions contribute constructively, others destructively.

## Nonlinear Wave Equation

> **[RED BOX — derivation]**

Starting from Maxwell's equations in a material where P depends nonlinearly on E:

**Step 1:** Start from ∇ × **H** = ∂**D**/∂t (Ampere's law with no free current)

**Step 2:** Take the curl: ∇ × (∇ × **E**) = -μ₀ ∂/∂t (∇ × **H**)

**Step 3:** Use the vector identity ∇ × (∇ × **E**) = ∇(∇·**E**) − ∇²**E**

**Step 4:** The key difference from the linear case: substitute **D** = ε₀**E** + **P** to separate linear and nonlinear parts

**Step 5:** Write **P** = **P_L** + **P_NL** (linear + nonlinear polarisation)

**Step 6:** The linear part gets absorbed into ε (modifies n), leaving:

> **[KEY EQUATION]**

$$\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} - \frac{n^2}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} = \frac{1}{\varepsilon_0 c^2}\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{P}_{NL}}{\partial t^2}$$

This is the **nonlinear wave equation**. It is identical to the linear wave equation except for the **source term** on the right — the second derivative of the nonlinear polarisation.

> **Physical interpretation:** The nonlinear polarisation P_NL acts as a **source** that drives new frequency components. The nonlinear material is like an antenna that receives the fundamental and re-radiates at the harmonic frequencies.

**Difference from linear wave equation:** In the linear case, P_NL = 0 and the right-hand side is zero. In the nonlinear case, P_NL oscillates at new frequencies and drives the generation of new fields.

## Coupled Amplitude Equations

To solve the nonlinear wave equation for a specific process (e.g., SFG), we write all fields in the form:

$$E_j(z,t) = A_j(z) e^{i(k_j z - \omega_j t)} + \text{c.c.}$$

Where **A_j(z) is the slowly-varying amplitude** — it changes over macroscopic distances (not on the optical cycle timescale).

**Slowly Varying Envelope Approximation (SVEA):** We assume:

$$\left|\frac{d^2 A_j}{dz^2}\right| \ll \left|k_j \frac{dA_j}{dz}\right|$$

The second derivative of the amplitude is negligible compared to the first derivative. This allows us to drop the ∂²A/∂z² term.

**Substituting into the nonlinear wave equation** (for SFG ω₃ = ω₁ + ω₂):

The **coupled amplitude equation** for the SFG field is:

> **[RED BOX — derivation]**

$$\frac{dA_3}{dz} = \frac{i\omega_3 d_{eff}}{n_3 c} A_1 A_2 e^{i\Delta k z}$$

And conversely for the input fields:

$$\frac{dA_1}{dz} = \frac{i\omega_1 d_{eff}}{n_1 c} A_3 A_2^* e^{-i\Delta k z}$$

$$\frac{dA_2}{dz} = \frac{i\omega_2 d_{eff}}{n_2 c} A_3 A_1^* e^{-i\Delta k z}$$

Where d_eff is the effective nonlinear coefficient (related to χ⁽²⁾ for a fixed geometry — scalar approximation).

**Key point:** The A fields are coupled — as ω₁ and ω₂ generate ω₃, the input fields are depleted.

> *"The trickiest step: remembering that A_j is a function of z, so when you differentiate E_j with respect to z, you need to use the product rule (chain rule). Forgetting this is how people lose marks."*

---

## Phase Matching Condition — Implications

Integrating the coupled amplitude equation (with undepleted pump approximation A₁, A₂ ≈ constant):

$$A_3(L) \propto \int_0^L e^{i\Delta k z} dz = L \cdot \text{sinc}\left(\frac{\Delta k L}{2}\right) e^{i\Delta k L/2}$$

The intensity of the generated signal:

> **[RED BOX]**

$$I_3 \propto L^2 \text{sinc}^2\left(\frac{\Delta k L}{2}\right)$$

Where sinc(x) = sin(x)/x.

**sinc² behaviour:**
- **Δk = 0:** sinc²(0) = 1 → maximum signal → grows as L² → **phase matched**
- **ΔkL/2 = π:** sinc²(π/2) → signal goes to zero → complete destructive interference

> Signal falls to zero when ΔkL/2 = π, i.e., when L = π/Δk. This is the **coherence length** L_c = π/Δk.

**Historical note:** Franken's 1961 SHG experiment was weak partly because he hadn't considered phase matching. Later, rotating the crystal revealed that the signal modulated strongly with angle, leading to the discovery of phase matching requirements.
