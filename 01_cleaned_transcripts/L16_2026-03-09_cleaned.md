# L16 — CARS and SRS: Coherent Raman Microscopy

**Date:** 9 March 2026 | **Slot:** Monday 13:32

---

## Motivation: Speed Limit of Spontaneous Raman

Spontaneous Raman is ~10⁶ times weaker than fluorescence:
- Fluorescence microscopy: ~1 µs per pixel → megapixel images in seconds
- Spontaneous Raman: ~1–10 s per pixel → megapixel image would take **days**

You can't simply increase laser power by 10⁶× — that would destroy the sample.

Nonlinear optics offers a path: **coherent Raman** — enhance the Raman signal coherently, achieving video-rate chemical imaging.

---

## CARS — Coherent Anti-Stokes Raman Scattering

**Historical note:** First demonstrated for imaging by Andreas Zumbusch (then at Caltech) in 1999 (Phys. Rev. Lett.). The four-wave mixing effect itself had been known since the 1960s (originally demonstrated by researchers at the Ford Motor Company, who didn't call it CARS).

**Process:** Third-order (χ⁽³⁾) four-wave mixing: 2ω_P − ω_S = ω_AS

- **ω_P** = pump frequency (laser 1)
- **ω_S** = Stokes frequency (laser 2, lower frequency than pump)
- **ω_AS** = anti-Stokes signal (output, higher frequency than pump)

**Tune for vibrational resonance:** Choose ω_P − ω_S = ω_vib (the vibrational frequency you want to image).

### Physical Picture

When ω_P and ω_S are present simultaneously, they create a **beating field**:

$$E_{beat} \propto \cos[(\omega_P - \omega_S)t]$$

If ω_P − ω_S = ω_vib, this beating drives the molecular vibration coherently. All molecules in the focal volume vibrate **in phase** (coherently), and then a second pump photon stimulates the anti-Stokes emission.

The coherent driving increases the signal by a factor of ~N (number of molecules coherently driven) — equivalent to the spontaneous Raman signal × N fold improvement.

## CARS Wavelength Calculation

> **[RED BOX — calculation]**

Given pump wavelength λ_P and vibrational wavenumber $\tilde{\nu}_{vib}$, find Stokes wavelength λ_S:

$$\tilde{\nu}_{vib} = \frac{1}{\lambda_P} - \frac{1}{\lambda_S}$$

Rearranging:

$$\lambda_S = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{\lambda_P} - \tilde{\nu}_{vib}}$$

**Note:** λ must be in cm if $\tilde{\nu}$ is in cm⁻¹ (or convert consistently).

Anti-Stokes wavelength λ_AS:

$$\frac{1}{\lambda_{AS}} = \frac{2}{\lambda_P} - \frac{1}{\lambda_S} = \frac{1}{\lambda_P} + \tilde{\nu}_{vib}$$

$$\lambda_{AS} = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{\lambda_P} + \tilde{\nu}_{vib}}$$

**Sanity checks:**
- λ_S > λ_P (Stokes is red-shifted from pump) ✓
- λ_AS < λ_P (anti-Stokes is blue-shifted from pump) ✓
- |λ_P − λ_AS| ≈ |λ_P − λ_S| (symmetric shift, approximately) ✓

**Example:** CH₂ stretch at 2900 cm⁻¹, pump at 800 nm:

$$\lambda_S = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{800\times10^{-7}\text{ cm}} - 2900\text{ cm}^{-1}} = \frac{1}{12500 - 2900}\text{ cm} = \frac{1}{9600}\text{ cm} \approx 1042\text{ nm}$$

$$\lambda_{AS} = \frac{1}{12500 + 2900}\text{ cm} = \frac{1}{15400}\text{ cm} \approx 649\text{ nm}$$

## Problem with CARS: Non-Resonant Background

**The electronic background:** The same 4WM process (2ω_P − ω_S) can arise through a **non-resonant electronic contribution** — shaking electrons at the frequency 2ω_P − ω_S regardless of whether there's a vibrational resonance. Since everything contains electrons, this gives an unwanted background in every pixel.

The background is:
- Always present (can't avoid it)
- Spectrally overlapping with the resonant CARS signal
- Especially problematic for molecules that give weak vibrational signals

---

## SRS — Stimulated Raman Scattering

**Principle:** Instead of detecting the anti-Stokes signal, detect the stimulated emission at the **Stokes frequency**.

When pump (ω_P) and Stokes (ω_S) are both present with ω_P − ω_S = ω_vib:
- **Stimulated Raman Gain (SRG):** The Stokes beam is amplified — extra photons are added at ω_S
- **Stimulated Raman Loss (SRL):** The pump beam is depleted — photons are removed from ω_P

The process is: ω_P → ω_S + phonon (molecule in first vibrational state)

This is the vibrational analogue of stimulated emission in a laser.

**Key advantage:** SRS has **no non-resonant electronic background**. The signal only appears when the vibrational resonance is matched.

### Detection: Modulation Transfer

1. Modulate the Stokes beam on/off at frequency f_mod (e.g., 20 MHz)
2. Detect the pump beam intensity on a photodiode
3. Use a **lock-in amplifier** tuned to f_mod to measure the tiny modulation on the pump signal

When Stokes is ON: pump is partially depleted at ω_vib resonance → dip at f_mod  
When Stokes is OFF: no depletion → no modulation at f_mod

The lock-in amplifier amplifies only signals at f_mod → extracts the tiny Raman signal from the large pump background.

> *Is SRS parametric or non-parametric?*  
> Non-parametric — the molecule is left in the first excited vibrational state (quantum state has changed). Therefore: no phase matching constraints, and Gouy phase doesn't cause destructive interference.

---

## CARS vs. SRS Comparison

| Property | CARS | SRS |
|----------|------|-----|
| Process | 4WM: 2ω_P − ω_S → ω_AS | Direct stimulation at ω_S |
| Background | Non-resonant electronic background | **Background free** |
| Signal direction | New wavelength generated (anti-Stokes, blue-shifted) | Same wavelengths as input (detect gain/loss) |
| Parametric? | Yes (χ⁽³⁾) | No (non-parametric) |
| Phase matching? | Some consideration | No |
| Sensitivity | Good but limited by NR background | Better for weak signals |
| Detection | Spectrometer or bandpass filter | Lock-in amplifier |

## Pulse Requirements for Coherent Raman

**For 2PEF, SHG, THG:** Want shortest pulses → highest peak intensity → use ~100 fs pulses.

**For CARS/SRS:** Must match the bandwidth of the pulse to the **Raman linewidth** (~10–20 cm⁻¹ ≈ few GHz).

Raman linewidths are set by the vibrational dephasing time (~picoseconds). For a 100 fs pulse:

$$\Delta\tilde{\nu} \approx \frac{c}{c\cdot\Delta t} = \frac{1}{(3\times10^{10})(10^{-13})} \approx 330 \text{ cm}^{-1}$$

At 330 cm⁻¹ bandwidth, you'd excite ~30 adjacent Raman peaks simultaneously — losing **chemical selectivity**.

> **[RED BOX]** For coherent Raman (CARS/SRS), use **picosecond pulses** to match the Raman linewidth and maintain spectral selectivity.

For ~1–5 ps pulses: bandwidth ≈ 1–5 cm⁻¹ → can resolve individual Raman peaks ✓

## Applications: Lipid Imaging

**Why lipids?** Lipids are small molecules — fluorescent labelling would perturb them too much (transit van on an elephant). But they have a strong, characteristic CH₂ symmetric stretch at **2845 cm⁻¹** and CH₂ asymmetric at **2900 cm⁻¹**.

**Examples:**
- Lipid droplets in cancer cells (cancer metabolism)
- Myelin sheaths insulating neurons in brain tissue
- Sebaceous glands in skin
- Plant cell walls (live imaging of infection response at 7 fps)

**Brain imaging demonstration:** 210 million pixels covering the whole mouse brain, acquired in ~30 minutes — comparable speed to fluorescence microscopy.
