# L14 — Time-Bandwidth Product and Start of Nonlinear Optical Microscopy

**Date:** 2 March 2026 | **Slot:** Monday 13:32

---

## FWHM for Intensity Gaussian

The intensity profile of a Gaussian pulse: I(t) = I₀ exp(−t²/2σ²)

At FWHM: I = I₀/2

$$\frac{1}{2} = e^{-t_{1/2}^2 / 2\sigma^2} \implies t_{1/2} = \sigma\sqrt{2\ln 2}$$

$$\text{FWHM} = 2t_{1/2} = 2\sigma\sqrt{2\ln 2}$$

> **[COMMON MISTAKE]** Make sure you're working with the *intensity* Gaussian (not the *amplitude* Gaussian). The amplitude has σ; the intensity Gaussian from squaring has σ/√2.

---

## Time-Bandwidth Product (TBP) — Precise Derivation

The uncertainty principle (Δν·Δt ≥ 1/4π) gives an approximate relationship. For a **Gaussian-shaped pulse**, the Fourier transform gives the exact result.

> **[RED BOX — derivation]**

**Step 1:** Amplitude pulse in time domain:
$$E(t) = E_0 e^{-t^2/2\sigma_t^2}$$

**Step 2:** Fourier transform of Gaussian (given in exam if needed):
$$\tilde{E}(\nu) \propto e^{-2\pi^2\nu^2\sigma_t^2}$$

This is a Gaussian in frequency with standard deviation σ_ν = 1/(2πσ_t).

**Step 3:** Convert to intensity and find FWHM in both time and frequency:

For intensity: I(t) ∝ |E(t)|² = E₀²e^{-t²/σ_t²}

$$\Delta t = 2\sigma_t\sqrt{\ln 2}$$

$$\Delta\nu = \frac{\sqrt{\ln 2}}{\pi\sigma_t}$$

**Step 4:** Multiply:

$$\Delta\nu \cdot \Delta t = \frac{\sqrt{\ln 2}}{\pi\sigma_t} \cdot 2\sigma_t\sqrt{\ln 2} = \frac{2\ln 2}{\pi} \approx 0.441$$

> **[RED BOX]**

$$\Delta\nu \cdot \Delta t = \frac{2\ln 2}{\pi} \approx 0.441 \quad \text{(Gaussian pulses)}$$

This is the **time-bandwidth product** for Gaussian-shaped transform-limited pulses. "Transform-limited" means the pulse has the shortest possible duration for its bandwidth (no extra chirp).

**Application:** Given a pulse duration, calculate minimum bandwidth (or vice versa):

For Δt = 1 ps at 800 nm:
$$\Delta\nu = \frac{0.441}{10^{-12}} = 4.41\times10^{11} \text{ Hz} = 441 \text{ GHz}$$

$$\Delta\lambda = \frac{\lambda^2}{c}\Delta\nu = \frac{(800\times10^{-9})^2}{3\times10^8} \times 4.41\times10^{11} \approx 0.94 \text{ nm}$$

## Physical Picture: Pulse Formation via Interference

A short pulse is formed by the **coherent superposition of many frequency components**.

When all frequency components have their peaks aligned at t=0:
- At t=0: all add constructively → peak intensity
- For t ≠ 0: different frequencies drift out of phase → destructive interference → intensity falls to zero

Wider bandwidth → faster dephasing → shorter pulse.

> This physically explains why more bandwidth → shorter pulse, and why the time-bandwidth product is constant.

## Chirp Compensation

**Problem:** Passing pulses through any dispersive material (glass, microscope objective lenses, crystal) stretches pulses via GVD.

**Solution:** Grating pairs or prism pairs inside the laser cavity (or before the experiment).

**Grating pair:** The two gratings separate the different wavelength components geometrically. By setting the angle between them, the blue light follows a shorter path and the red light follows a longer path — compensating the opposite effect of GVD (where blue travels slower → arrives later → can give it a head start).

> Be able to explain the principle qualitatively and sketch the grating pair diagram.

## Introduction to Nonlinear Optical Microscopy

Now we bring everything together. This is "where it all starts to come together."

### Review: Limitations of Conventional Confocal Fluorescence

1. **Requires fluorescent labels** — not always valid (small molecules, in-vivo)
2. **Pinhole needed** — but scattering defeats pinhole detection (scattered photons miss the pinhole)
3. **Scattering limits depth** — typically < 100 µm in tissue
4. **Blue/UV excitation** — high scattering, photodamage
5. **Spatial resolution vs. scattering tradeoff** (d ∝ λ, I_scatter ∝ 1/λ⁴)

### Key Advantage of Nonlinear Excitation

**Signal scales with intensity² (or intensity³)**:

$$S_{2PEF} \propto I^2 \quad S_{THG} \propto I^3$$

Since the intensity is only very high **right at the focus** (the diffraction-limited spot), the signal is only generated at the focal volume — **everywhere else, the intensity is too low** for a nonlinear response.

This is **intrinsically confocal** — no pinhole needed.

## Two-Photon Excited Fluorescence (2PEF)

**Process:** Two infrared photons (each at wavelength 2λ_ex) are simultaneously absorbed; their combined energy equals one UV/visible photon of wavelength λ_ex. The molecule then fluoresces normally.

**Jablonski diagram for 2PEF:**
- Two red photons absorbed simultaneously → ground state to excited electronic state (same transition as 1-photon UV absorption)
- Vibrational relaxation (wavy arrows)
- Fluorescence emission (green photon)

**Advantages over 1PEF:**

1. **No pinhole needed** — signal only from focal volume (I² dependence)
2. **Deeper penetration** — excitation at 2× wavelength → Rayleigh scattering reduced by factor 2⁴ = **16**
3. **Lower photodamage** — IR photons less damaging than UV/blue
4. **Can image deep** — demonstrated up to ~800 µm into mouse brain

The famous Amos photograph: one cuvette of fluorescent dye excited by blue laser (showing the full hourglass cone of fluorescence) vs. the same dye excited by pulsed IR (showing only a tiny point of fluorescence at the focus).

> *"This was a groundbreaking demonstration. It shows with your own eyes why nonlinear excitation gives you intrinsic optical sectioning."*

**Depth penetration example:** 2PEF imaging of mouse brain neurons to ~800 µm depth.

Three-photon excitation (3PEF) extends this further (~1 mm+).

## Pump-Probe / Transient Absorption Microscopy

**Alternative nonlinear approach to imaging absorbing chromophores** (without fluorescence):

Two colour pulses: **pump** (ω₁) + **probe** (ω₂), where ω₁ + ω₂ = absorption transition energy.

**Detection:** Modulate the pump beam on/off at frequency f_mod. Detect the **modulation on the probe** beam intensity using a **lock-in amplifier** tuned to f_mod.

When pump is ON: two-photon absorption depletes probe → dip in probe intensity.  
When pump is OFF: no dip.

The lock-in amplifier measures only the signal at f_mod — rejects all other noise.

**Application:** Label-free imaging of **hemoglobin** in blood cells:
- Tune ω₁ + ω₂ to hemoglobin absorption band (~415 nm or 540 nm Soret/Q bands)
- Image red blood cells in a 3D volume (400×400×300 µm mouse brain vasculature)
- No fluorescent labels needed

> Green box — understand principle but no calculation needed.
