## 1. Electromagnetism Recap

### Electric Field

$$\mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{E}$$

The electric field tells you the force a charge would experience. Units: **N C⁻¹** (equivalently V m⁻¹).

### Polarisation Vector P

$$\mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0 \chi \mathbf{E}$$

- **P** = induced dipole moment per unit volume (units: C m⁻²)
- **χ** = electric susceptibility
- In isotropic materials χ is a scalar; in general it is a tensor (matrix that maps E onto P)
- χ is a function of **frequency** — it tells us how the material responds to different wavelengths

> *Matthew probably glossed over this and assumed it was a scalar, but in reality it's a tensor.*

### Displacement Field D

$$\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P}$$

D separates **free charge** from **bound charge** contributions. This is a workaround allowing us to deal with dielectric materials (insulators with some free and some bound charge). We'll use D later in the nonlinear wave equation. Units: same as P, C m⁻².

If χ = 0: **D = ε₀E**.

> We're primarily interested in the **bound charge** polarisation P — that's the part that tells us about the material's properties.

### Maxwell's Equations and the Wave Equation

You should all be familiar with how to derive the wave equation from Maxwell's equations:
1. Take the curl of ∇ × **E** = -∂**B**/∂t
2. Use the vector identity ∇ × (∇ × **E**) = ∇(∇·**E**) − ∇²**E**
3. In free space with no charges: ∇·**E** = 0, so ∇²**E** = μ₀ε₀ ∂²**E**/∂t²

This is the **wave equation** — confirms that free space supports the propagation of electromagnetic waves at speed c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀).

> *Later we'll modify this derivation by substituting D for E, which allows us to separate the nonlinear polarisation P and get the nonlinear wave equation.*

### Intensity and Electric Field

> **[RED BOX]** Must remember:

$$I = \frac{1}{2} c \varepsilon_0 |E|^2$$

Intensity (W m⁻²) scales with the **square** of the electric field amplitude. This is the Poynting vector magnitude averaged over a cycle.

## 2. Harmonic Motion (Waves & Optics Recap)

Think of pushing someone on a swing — the classic harmonic oscillator:

- At **very low driving frequency** (ω → 0): small static displacement, in phase with force
- At **resonance** (ω = ω₀): maximum displacement, force is 90° out of phase with displacement — this is where you push at exactly the right moment as they swing back
- At **very high driving frequency** (ω ≫ ω₀): the system can't respond — near-zero displacement, 180° out of phase

**Equation of motion for a driven damped harmonic oscillator:**

$$m\ddot{r} + m\gamma\dot{r} + m\omega_0^2 r = qE_0 e^{-i\omega t}$$

Where:
- m = mass of the particle
- γ = damping coefficient
- ω₀ = resonant frequency = √(k/m)
- qE₀e^{-iωt} = driving force from the electromagnetic field

> *We'll be modelling charges bound to atoms as masses on a spring — this forms the basis of the Lorentz oscillator model in a few lectures' time.*

## 3. Quantum Mechanics Recap

### Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

You know the position-momentum version. The one we'll use most is:

> **[RED BOX]**

$$\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

**Physical meaning for laser pulses:** If we squeeze photons into very short pulses (small Δt), the uncertainty in their energy (ΔE) shoots up. Since E = hf, this means the pulse has a broad range of frequencies — it has a **wide bandwidth**.

> *This is why ultrashort pulses have a broad spectrum. We'll derive this more precisely using Fourier transforms.*

### Bohr Model and Atomic Radius

The Bohr model treats the hydrogen atom as an electron orbiting a positive nucleus. Balance centripetal force with Coulomb force, using quantised angular momentum (mvr = nℏ):

$$a_0 = \frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0\hbar^2}{me^2} \approx 0.5 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m} \approx 0.5 \text{ Å}$$

This is the Bohr radius — the scale of atoms (~10⁻¹⁰ m).

**Atomic electric field (important for estimating nonlinear thresholds):**

$$E_{at} = \frac{e}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 a_0^2} \approx 5 \times 10^{11} \text{ V m}^{-1}$$

> *This is the electric field the electron experiences in a hydrogen atom. We'll use this as the threshold for estimating when nonlinear effects become significant.*

### Hydrogen Absorption Energy

Energy levels of hydrogen:

$$E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}$$

For the ground state → first excited state transition (n=1 → n=2):

$$\Delta E = 13.6 \left(1 - \frac{1}{4}\right) = 10.2 \text{ eV}$$

Converting to wavelength using E = hc/λ:

$$\lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{hc}{10.2 \text{ eV}} \approx 122 \text{ nm}$$

This is in the **extreme UV** — not useful for optical imaging. Everything absorbs in this region, and it's destructive to biological samples. So simple atomic hydrogen absorption is not the contrast mechanism we want.

### Infinite Square Potential Well

From quantum mechanics (Claire's module), an electron confined in a 1D box of width L has energy levels:

$$E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}$$

For absorption in the **visible range** (green light, λ ≈ 500 nm):

$$\Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = \frac{3h^2}{8mL^2} = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

Solving for L:

$$L = \sqrt{\frac{3h\lambda}{8mc}} \approx 6.7 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m} \approx 0.67 \text{ nm}$$

> *This is about 7 atomic bond lengths — larger than a single atom! So optical absorption in the visible requires electrons delocalised over multiple atomic bonds (conjugated pi-bond systems). This is why only special molecules are coloured.*

## 4. Gaussian Functions

Gaussian functions are used throughout this course to:
- Approximate the intensity profile of a focused laser spot
- Model the time profile of ultrafast laser pulses

A Gaussian is defined by its **standard deviation σ** but in optics we measure the **Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM)**:

> **[RED BOX]** Must be able to derive and remember:

$$\text{FWHM} = 2\sqrt{2\ln 2} \cdot \sigma \approx 2.355\sigma$$

**Derivation:** At the FWHM, intensity I = I₀/2:

$$\frac{1}{2} = e^{-x_{1/2}^2 / 2\sigma^2}$$

$$\ln\frac{1}{2} = -\frac{x_{1/2}^2}{2\sigma^2} \implies x_{1/2} = \sigma\sqrt{2\ln 2}$$

$$\text{FWHM} = 2x_{1/2} = 2\sigma\sqrt{2\ln 2}$$

> *This derivation is standard — we'll use it several times, especially for pulse width and spatial resolution.*

## 5. Fourier Transforms

The Fourier transform converts between the **time domain** and the **frequency domain** (or equivalently, space ↔ spatial frequency).

$$\tilde{E}(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E(t) e^{i\omega t} \, dt$$

**Key results:**
- A single-frequency sine wave → a single spike (delta function) in frequency space
- Two frequencies mixed together → two spikes
- A short pulse (broad in time width) → broad in frequency

**The most important standard result:**

> **[RED BOX]** The Fourier transform of a Gaussian is a Gaussian (given in exam if needed):

$$\mathcal{F}\{e^{-t^2/2\sigma^2}\} \propto e^{-\omega^2 \sigma^2/2}$$

Key observation: **wider in time → narrower in frequency; narrower in time → wider in frequency.** This is the mathematical expression of the uncertainty principle.

> *If you ever need to do a Fourier transform in the exam, the standard formula will be given on the front page. What's assessed is how to apply it.*

**Bandwidth conversion warning:**

$$\Delta\lambda = \frac{\lambda^2}{c} \Delta f$$

This is NOT a linear relationship — you cannot simply convert Δf to Δλ by substituting λ = c/f directly. You must differentiate f = c/λ:

$$\frac{df}{d\lambda} = -\frac{c}{\lambda^2} \implies |\Delta\lambda| = \frac{\lambda^2}{c} |\Delta f|$$

> **[COMMON MISTAKE]** People forget to use this conversion and just say Δf ∝ Δλ. They are **not** proportional — they are inversely related, and the central wavelength appears in the conversion factor.
