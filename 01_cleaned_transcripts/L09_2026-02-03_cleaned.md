# L09 — Perturbation Theory: Deriving Nonlinear Susceptibilities

**Date:** 3 February 2026 | **Slot:** Tuesday 12:32

---

> *"This is probably the trickiest or most mathematical part of the course — but nothing is conceptually difficult. It's just solving differential equations systematically."*

---

## Modified Equation of Motion

When the restoring force is no longer linear (anharmonic potential), the equation of motion becomes a **nonlinear ODE**.

### Non-centrosymmetric Material (χ⁽²⁾ ≠ 0)

Modified restoring force with an asymmetric quadratic correction:

$$m\ddot{r} + m\gamma\dot{r} + m\omega_0^2 r + ma r^2 = qE(t)$$

The new term **ma r²** introduces the nonlinearity. Parameter *a* controls the strength of the non-linearity.

### Centrosymmetric Material (χ⁽²⁾ = 0)

Modified restoring force with a symmetric cubic correction:

$$m\ddot{r} + m\gamma\dot{r} + m\omega_0^2 r + mb r^3 = qE(t)$$

The new term **mb r³** is symmetric (same in both directions of displacement).

---

## Perturbation Theory Method

> **[RED BOX — technique]**

Standard approach for nonlinear ODEs: assume the solution can be expanded as a power series in a small parameter λ (tuning parameter, 0 ≤ λ ≤ 1):

$$r = \lambda r_1 + \lambda^2 r_2 + \lambda^3 r_3 + \cdots$$

Replace E with λE. The idea: λ = 1 at the end, so this is just a mathematical trick to separate orders. λ characterises the strength of the perturbation (how nonlinear the system is).

**Key principle:** Each term in the equation of motion proportional to λⁿ must be satisfied independently. This separates the one nonlinear ODE into a set of linear ODEs for each order.

---

## Non-Centrosymmetric Case: Solving Order by Order

**First-order equation (λ¹ terms):** (picking out only λ¹ terms from the expanded equation)

$$m\ddot{r}_1 + m\gamma\dot{r}_1 + m\omega_0^2 r_1 = qE_0 e^{-i\omega t}$$

This is **identical to the linear Lorentz oscillator** — so first-order response is the same as the linear case. ✓

**Second-order equation (λ² terms):** (picking out only λ² terms)

$$m\ddot{r}_2 + m\gamma\dot{r}_2 + m\omega_0^2 r_2 = -ma r_1^2$$

Note: the right-hand side contains **r₁²** (the square of the linear response). The original driving force disappears and **r₁² becomes the new driving force** for the second-order response.

This makes physical sense: the linear oscillation drives the nonlinear term, which in turn drives the second-order displacement.

**Third-order equation (λ³ terms):**

$$m\ddot{r}_3 + m\gamma\dot{r}_3 + m\omega_0^2 r_3 = -2ma r_1 r_2$$

The driving force is now a combination of first and second order terms.

---

## Deriving χ⁽²⁾ for SHG

**Choose the second-harmonic solution:** We look for a solution at frequency 2ω:

$$r_2 = r_2^{(2\omega)} e^{-2i\omega t}$$

Substituting into the second-order equation of motion and isolating the coefficient at 2ω:

$$r_2^{(2\omega)} = \frac{-a \, r_1^{(ω) \, 2}}{(-4\omega^2 - 2i\gamma\omega + \omega_0^2)} = \frac{-a \, r_1^{(\omega) 2}}{D(2\omega)}$$

Where D(2ω) = ω₀² − 4ω² − 2iγω is the **detuning factor at 2ω**.

**Steps to χ⁽²⁾:**
1. r₁⁽ω⁾ = first-order (linear) displacement at ω = qE₀/m × 1/D(ω)
2. Dipole moment: μ⁽²⁾ = q × r₂
3. Polarizability: α⁽²⁾ = μ⁽²⁾/E²
4. χ⁽²⁾(2ω; ω,ω) = Nα⁽²⁾ (summed over N atoms per unit volume)

**Result:** χ⁽²⁾ has the form:

$$\chi^{(2)}(2\omega;\omega,\omega) \propto \frac{-a \, q^3/m^2}{D(\omega)^2 D(2\omega)}$$

> *The key is understanding the method, not memorising the final expression. In an exam you'd be asked to derive the second-order susceptibility for a specific process (e.g., SHG, SFG).*

**Other second-order processes** (same method, different solution frequency chosen):
- SFG at ω₁ + ω₂: try solution r₂ ∝ e^{-i(ω₁+ω₂)t}
- DFG at ω₁ − ω₂: try solution r₂ ∝ e^{-i(ω₁-ω₂)t}
- Optical rectification (DC): try solution r₂ = const

> **[EXAM HINT]** You will be asked to derive the second-order susceptibility for SHG, SFG, or DFG. The steps are the same — the only difference is which frequency you seek in the solution.

## Centrosymmetric Case: Why χ⁽²⁾ = 0

For the centrosymmetric case (r³ term instead of r²):

**Second-order equation (λ² terms):** When we expand r³, we need to find terms proportional to λ². Looking at r³ = (λr₁ + λ²r₂ + ...)³:

- Terms in λ²: would require finding r₁² from the r³ term — but r³ gives λ³r₁³ + 3λ⁴r₁²r₂ + ...
- There are **no terms proportional to λ²** in r³ — the lowest contribution from r³ is λ³.

Therefore: the second-order equation of motion has **zero on the right-hand side**. No driving term → **no second-order response** → χ⁽²⁾ = 0.

**Third-order equation (λ³ terms):**

$$m\ddot{r}_3 + m\gamma\dot{r}_3 + m\omega_0^2 r_3 = -mb r_1^3$$

The driving term is now r₁³ — the linear response cubed.

> **This mathematically confirms the symmetry argument**: centrosymmetric materials have no χ⁽²⁾ but do have χ⁽³⁾.

## Summary: What to Know

**Things you need to be able to do:**
1. Write down the modified equation of motion (with r² for non-centrosymmetric, r³ for centrosymmetric)
2. Apply the perturbation technique: replace E → λE, expand r as power series in λ
3. Separate into first, second, third-order equations by collecting terms in λ, λ², λ³
4. Solve the second-order equation for SHG (look for solution at 2ω)
5. Show that centrosymmetric materials give χ⁽²⁾ = 0

**Things you don't need to do:**
- Derive χ⁽³⁾ for specific processes (too much writing for an exam)
- Know all 44 frequency components of the third-order response
