## The Nonlinear Power Series

The fundamental equation P = ε₀χE is linear in E. For high field strengths, the response becomes nonlinear. We express this as a **power series**:

> **[RED BOX]**

$$\mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0\left[\chi^{(1)}\mathbf{E} + \chi^{(2)}\mathbf{E}^2 + \chi^{(3)}\mathbf{E}^3 + \cdots\right]$$

$$= \mathbf{P}^{(1)} + \mathbf{P}^{(2)} + \mathbf{P}^{(3)} + \cdots$$

Each order can be treated separately:
- P⁽¹⁾ = ε₀χ⁽¹⁾E (linear response — refraction, absorption)
- P⁽²⁾ = ε₀χ⁽²⁾E² (second-order — SHG, SFG, DFG)
- P⁽³⁾ = ε₀χ⁽³⁾E³ (third-order — THG, CARS, two-photon absorption)

---

## Back-of-Envelope Estimates for χ⁽²⁾ and χ⁽³⁾

> **[RED BOX — calculation method]**

Assumption: the nonlinear response becomes of the same order as the linear response when E ≈ E_at (the atomic field strength ≈ 5×10¹¹ V/m).

At E = E_at: P⁽¹⁾ ≈ P⁽²⁾:

$$\varepsilon_0\chi^{(1)}E_{at} \approx \varepsilon_0\chi^{(2)}E_{at}^2$$

Since χ⁽¹⁾ ≈ 1 (for typical solid-state materials):

$$\chi^{(2)} \approx \frac{\chi^{(1)}}{E_{at}} = \frac{1}{5\times10^{11}} \approx 2\times10^{-12} \text{ m/V}$$

Similarly, P⁽¹⁾ ≈ P⁽³⁾ gives:

$$\chi^{(3)} \approx \frac{1}{E_{at}^2} \approx 4\times10^{-24} \text{ m}^2/\text{V}^2$$

> These are **incredibly small**. This is why you don't see nonlinear effects in everyday life — you need extreme laser intensities to drive them.

## Units of χ: Dimensional Analysis

> **[RED BOX]**

| Order | χ units | Why |
|-------|---------|-----|
| χ⁽¹⁾ | **dimensionless** | P = ε₀χ⁽¹⁾E; ε₀ and E have units that combine to give C m⁻² for P |
| χ⁽²⁾ | **m V⁻¹** | P = ε₀χ⁽²⁾E²; extra factor of E (V/m) in denominator |
| χ⁽³⁾ | **m² V⁻²** | P = ε₀χ⁽³⁾E³; two extra factors of E |

> **[COMMON MISTAKE]** Don't assume all orders of χ have the same units. They don't — each has an additional (V/m)⁻¹ per order.

Check: From P (C/m²) = ε₀ (C²/N·m²) × χ⁽¹⁾ × E (V/m = N/C):
χ⁽¹⁾ must be dimensionless. ✓  
χ⁽²⁾ must have units (V/m)⁻¹ = m/V ✓

## Origin of Nonlinear Response: Anharmonic Potential

In the Lorentz oscillator model, we assumed a **parabolic (harmonic) potential** (linear restoring force). This is valid for **small displacements**.

At high field strengths, the potential deviates from parabolic — the real potential looks more like a Lennard-Jones curve (anharmonic).

**Two cases:**

### Case 1: Non-centrosymmetric potential (χ⁽²⁾ ≠ 0)
The potential is **asymmetric** — easier to pull charges one way than the other. The restoring force is asymmetric. This gives rise to a distorted sine wave when driven (not symmetric top/bottom), which contains **even harmonics** (2ω).

### Case 2: Centrosymmetric potential (χ⁽²⁾ = 0)
The potential has a **centre of symmetry** — the same shape in both directions. The restoring force is symmetric. This gives rise to a distorted (peaky) but symmetric sine wave, containing **only odd harmonics** (3ω, 5ω, …).

**Why centrosymmetry kills χ⁽²⁾:**

For any second-order process: P⁽²⁾ = ε₀χ⁽²⁾E²

If E → −E (reverse the field sign): P⁽²⁾ should also reverse sign (as P is a physical displacement).
But (−E)² = E² → P⁽²⁾ stays **positive**.

So P⁽²⁾ cannot change sign when E changes sign → P⁽²⁾ must be zero in a centrosymmetric material.

> **[EXAM HINT]** This symmetry argument is examinable — know why χ⁽²⁾ = 0 for centrosymmetric materials, and why χ⁽³⁾ ≠ 0 for all materials.

**In practice:** Very few materials lack centrosymmetry — crystals like quartz (at certain orientations), BBO, lithium niobate. Biological structures like collagen and myosin also lack centrosymmetry (→ SHG imaging).

## Complex Conjugates and Negative Frequencies

Any real electromagnetic field can be written as:

$$E(t) = E_0 e^{-i\omega t} + E_0^* e^{+i\omega t} = E_0 e^{-i\omega t} + \text{c.c.}$$

The "c.c." (complex conjugate) notation packages up all the conjugate terms.

**Why this matters for nonlinear optics:** When we square or cube E to find higher-order polarisations, we must include the complex conjugates — they give rise to new frequency components including **sum**, **difference**, and **DC** terms.

For two fields E₁ and E₂:
$$E(t) = E_1 e^{-i\omega_1 t} + E_2 e^{-i\omega_2 t} + \text{c.c.}$$

---

## Second Harmonic Generation (SHG)

**History:** Demonstrated by Franken in 1961 — one year after the first laser.

For a single input field at frequency ω:

$$P^{(2)} \propto \chi^{(2)} E^2 = \chi^{(2)} (E_0 e^{-i\omega t} + \text{c.c.})^2$$

Expanding:

$$P^{(2)} \propto \chi^{(2)} \left[E_0^2 e^{-2i\omega t} + (E_0^*)^2 e^{+2i\omega t} + 2|E_0|^2\right]$$

Three terms:
1. **e^{−2iωt}** → oscillation at **2ω** = **second harmonic generation** (SHG)
2. **e^{+2iωt}** → complex conjugate of SHG
3. **2|E₀|²** → **DC term** = **optical rectification** (no oscillation)

Two photons at ω → one photon at 2ω (energy conserved: hω + hω = h·2ω).

---

## Sum and Difference Frequency Generation

For two input fields at ω₁ and ω₂:

$$P^{(2)} \propto \chi^{(2)} (E_1 e^{-i\omega_1 t} + E_2 e^{-i\omega_2 t} + \text{c.c.})^2$$

Expanding gives six terms at frequencies:
- **2ω₁** (SHG of ω₁)
- **2ω₂** (SHG of ω₂)
- **ω₁ + ω₂** (Sum Frequency Generation, SFG)
- **ω₁ − ω₂** (Difference Frequency Generation, DFG)
- **DC** (optical rectification, two terms)

---

## Third Harmonic Generation (THG)

For a single input:

$$P^{(3)} \propto \chi^{(3)} E^3 = \chi^{(3)} (E_0\cos\omega t)^3$$

Using cos³θ = (3cosθ + cos3θ)/4:

$$P^{(3)} \propto \chi^{(3)} \left[\frac{3}{4}E_0\cos\omega t + \frac{1}{4}E_0\cos 3\omega t\right]$$

Two terms:
1. **ω** (linear-like response at the fundamental)
2. **3ω** (third harmonic)

Three photons at ω → one photon at 3ω.

### General Third-Order: 44 Frequency Components

For three distinct input frequencies ω₁, ω₂, ω₃:

$$P^{(3)} \propto \chi^{(3)} (E_1 + E_2 + E_3 + \text{c.c.})^3$$

This produces **44 distinct frequency components** when all combinations of ±ω₁, ±ω₂, ±ω₃ are considered.

> *"I'm never going to ask you to produce all 44 in an exam — it would just take too long."*

The key ones to remember are highlighted in lectures:
- **Third harmonic generation:** 3ω₁
- **Four-wave mixing:** 2ω₁ − ω₂ (central to CARS microscopy — covered later)

> **[EXAM HINT]** The important ones in blue: **χ₃ is where the action is** (quote from the lecturer).
