# L03 — Optical Imaging Part 1: Contrast Mechanisms

**Date:** 19 January 2026 | **Slot:** Monday 13:32

---

This is the start of the actual topic — optical imaging starting with contrast.

> *This is my favourite lecture of the course.*

Recap: When we shine light on a material, what we see is mediated by χ (chi) — the electric susceptibility. Everything that gives rise to colour and contrast is hidden within χ.

**Why microscopy?** We focus on microscopy because:
- We need a tight diffraction-limited focus for nonlinear optics (to achieve the high intensities required)
- We're looking at small things (biological cells, molecules)

---

## Why Do We Need Imaging?

Three main reasons in science:
1. See things **too small** to see with the naked eye (e.g., zooplankton < 1 mm)
2. See things **too far away** (astronomy)
3. See things that **don't interact with visible light** — accessing other regions of the EM spectrum reveals properties invisible to our eyes

**Goal in biological imaging:** Understand the spatial distribution of molecules in tissues, their composition, structure, and how they interact and change in disease states. This is called **chemical contrast** or **chemomorphology**.

### Reflection vs. Transmission Imaging

- **Reflection:** White light illuminates sample, we see the reflected wavelengths (e.g., white paint reflects all wavelengths; black absorbs all)
- **Transmission:** Light passes through a stained glass window — we see what's *not* absorbed

### Examples of contrast in a natural image

A landscape photograph shows:
- **Absorption** (wavelength-selective): grass is green because chlorophyll absorbs red and blue
- **Scattering** (wavelength-dependent, Rayleigh): sky appears orange/red at sunset (I ∝ 1/λ⁴)
- **Reflection** (specular): pond reflects sky

## Types of Optical Contrast

### 1. Absorption

**Beer-Lambert Law:**

$$A = \log\left(\frac{I_0}{I_T}\right) = \varepsilon c l$$

Where:
- A = absorbance
- I₀ = incident intensity
- I_T = transmitted intensity
- ε = molar extinction coefficient (intrinsic property of the molecule)
- c = concentration
- l = path length through the sample

> Absorption depends on **both concentration and path length**. You cannot distinguish concentration changes from thickness changes unless one is controlled.

**Visible absorption** arises from electronic transitions (UV to visible range).  
**Infrared absorption** arises from molecular vibrations (atom-atom bond stretching/bending).

### 2. Fluorescence

Fluorescence requires molecules that can:
1. Absorb a photon (excite an electron from ground to excited state)
2. Lose some energy via **non-radiative vibrational relaxation** (heat)
3. Emit a photon at a **longer wavelength** (lower energy) — the **Stokes shift**

> **[RED BOX]** Be able to draw and label the **Jablonski diagram**:

```
  S₁ ─────────────────────────
     |  ← vibrational states (thin lines)
     |  non-radiative relaxation (wavy arrow, heat)
     ↓
  S₁(v=0) ─────────────────────
     |
     | fluorescence emission (solid downward arrow, photon out)
     ↓
  S₀ ─────────────────────────
```

- **Solid arrows** = optical transitions (photon absorbed or emitted)
- **Wavy arrows** = non-optical transitions (vibrational relaxation, energy → heat)

The emitted fluorescence photon has **lower energy (longer wavelength)** than the absorbed photon because energy was lost to vibrations.

**Phosphorescence:** Similar but involves an intermediate triplet state — much slower process (glow-in-the-dark materials).

**Demo:** Tonic water (quinine) fluoresces blue under UV light. Quinine absorbs UV but emits visible blue light.

> *If you drink gin and tonic under a club's UV (blacklight), the tonic glows. The quinine molecule has only two conjugated rings — too short to absorb visible light — but it does absorb UV and emits blue fluorescence.*

### 3. Scattering

Milk is white because:
- Tiny fat droplets suspended in water
- At each fat/water interface there is a refractive index mismatch → scattering
- Scattering is wavelength-independent here (Mie scattering for larger particles), giving white appearance

**Demo:** Tank of water with a small amount of milk added — blue light (488 nm) scatters much more visibly than red light (633 nm), demonstrating I ∝ 1/λ⁴.

## Physical Origin of Colour in Molecules

From the infinite square potential well calculation (L02): visible absorption requires electrons confined over L ≈ 0.67 nm (about 7 bond lengths).

**How do real molecules achieve this?** Through **conjugated π-bond systems**:

- Carbon–carbon double bonds (C=C) have **π orbitals** with electrons that can become delocalized
- When single and double bonds alternate (e.g., –C=C–C=C–), electrons are delocalised across the entire conjugated system
- The longer the conjugated chain, the larger L → the longer the wavelength absorbed → lower energy transition (further into visible/infrared)

**Examples:**
- **Chlorophyll** — large porphyrin ring with conjugated system; absorbs red and blue, reflects green (why plants are green)
- **Beta-carotene** — in carrots; long conjugated chain, absorbs blue/green, transmits orange
- **Fluorescein** — multiple conjugated rings; absorbs ~490 nm, emits ~520 nm

**Back-of-envelope calculation for fluorescein:**  
Using the 1D infinite potential well approximation, with peak absorption at ~490 nm:

$$L = \sqrt{\frac{3h\lambda}{8mc}} \approx 0.4 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m} = 0.4 \text{ nm}$$

This matches the physical size of the conjugated system in fluorescein measured by X-ray diffraction. 

> *Evolution has exploited quantum mechanics: plants produce conjugated molecules to absorb specific photon energies for photosynthesis and protection.*

**Indicators (e.g., litmus paper):** The pH changes the molecular structure slightly (attaches/removes an H), changing the effective length L of the conjugated system → colour change. It's quantum mechanics in a chemistry classroom experiment.

## Labelling for Contrast

Biological cells are largely colourless and transparent — very little contrast without labelling.

### Immunofluorescence Labelling

1. Take a **fluorescent dye** (like fluorescein)
2. Conjugate it to an **antibody** specific to a target molecule
3. The antibody binds selectively to its target in the cell
4. Illuminate with UV/blue light → target molecules light up in the colour of the fluorescent dye

Multiple dyes (different emission wavelengths but same excitation) allow imaging of different structures simultaneously in colour.

### H&E Staining (Haematoxylin & Eosin)

The **gold standard** in histopathology (cancer diagnosis):
- **Haematoxylin** stains chromatin (nuclei) **purple/blue**
- **Eosin (acid dye)** stains extracellular proteins and connective tissue **pink**

Path: tissue → fix in wax → slice thin section → remove wax → stain with H&E → image under microscope.

Pathologists trained to identify cancerous tissue from cell appearance and distribution.

> *Victorians developed this and it's still used today. We'll see how nonlinear optics can replicate H&E contrast without any staining.*

---

## Limitations of Labelling

**The elephant-GPS-tag analogy:**

- **Proteins** (~10,000–100,000 g/mol) + fluorescent tag (~350 g/mol): tag is tiny compared to protein (like a 1 kg GPS tag on a 5-tonne elephant) — acceptable perturbation
- **Small molecules** (drug molecules, fatty acids, ~100–500 g/mol) + fluorescent tag (~350 g/mol): tag is comparable in size to the molecule (like a Ford Transit van on the elephant) — **completely changes behaviour**

**Additional limitations:**
1. **Thin sections required** for transmission imaging (Beer-Lambert: need controlled path length)
2. **Hydrophobic environments** — water-based dyes can't penetrate fatty tissues; sample must be dehydrated first (changes tissue state)
3. **Clinical constraints** — can't fluorescently label a patient

**Conclusion:** Labelling is extremely powerful when applicable, but for small molecules, in-vivo imaging, or when you want to see the natural state of the tissue — you need **label-free** methods. This is where nonlinear optics comes in.
