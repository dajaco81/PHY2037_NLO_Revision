# L05 — Lorentz Oscillator Model and Linear Susceptibility χ(ω)

**Date:** 26 January 2026 | **Slot:** Monday 13:32

---

Recap from last week:
- We've looked at contrast mechanisms in imaging
- The key equation: P = ε₀χE, where χ contains everything we want to know
- We saw how material properties link to wavelength through electronic and vibrational transitions
- Now we're going to understand *how* χ depends on frequency by solving the equation of motion for charges in a material

---

## Goal

We want to derive the **electric susceptibility χ(ω)** — to show how easily a material can be polarised depends on the frequency of the applied electromagnetic field.

We'll use the **Lorentz oscillator model**, named after Dutch physicist Hendrik Lorentz.

> *"This is where waves and optics module (harmonic oscillators) meets electromagnetism."*

---

## Single Charge Radiation Model

Start with a thought experiment: a single point charge.

A stationary point charge has radial electric field lines pointing outward. If you **suddenly shift** that charge, continuity of field lines requires the field disturbance to propagate outward as a wave. If you **shake** the charge up and down sinusoidally, you get a sinusoidal electromagnetic wave propagating away from the charge — the charge is radiating.

> This is what happens in a material: the applied EM wave shakes the bound electrons, each of which then emits its own EM radiation. The combination of all these emitted fields is what we detect as the material response.

---

## Lorentz Oscillator Model

Model: electrons bound to positive nuclei in a dielectric behave like **masses on springs**.

**Physical picture:**
- Without field: electron cloud symmetrically distributed around positive nucleus
- With oscillating EM field: electrons are driven back and forth
- There is a **linear restoring force** (spring) pulling electrons back to equilibrium
- There is **damping** (friction with the surrounding material)

**This is only an approximation** — the parabolic (harmonic) potential is valid for **small displacements**. For large displacements (high field strengths), the potential becomes anharmonic → this is the origin of nonlinear optics.

### Equation of Motion

> **[RED BOX — derivation]**

$$m\ddot{r} + m\gamma\dot{r} + m\omega_0^2 r = qE_0 e^{-i\omega t}$$

Terms:
- **m r̈**: inertial force (mass × acceleration)
- **m γ ṙ**: damping force (proportional to velocity)
- **m ω₀² r**: restoring force (spring); ω₀ = √(k/m) is the resonant frequency
- **qE₀e^{-iωt}**: driving force from the EM field (electric force on the electron)

### Solution by Complex Exponential

Try the solution: r = r₀ e^{-iωt}

Substituting (noting ṙ = −iωr₀e^{-iωt}, r̈ = −ω²r₀e^{-iωt}):

$$(-m\omega^2 - im\gamma\omega + m\omega_0^2) r_0 = qE_0$$

Solving for r₀ (the displacement amplitude):

$$r_0 = \frac{qE_0/m}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) - i\gamma\omega}$$

### From Displacement to Susceptibility

**Step 1:** Electric dipole moment of a single atom:
$$\mu = q \cdot r$$

**Step 2:** Polarizability of a single atom α (ratio of dipole moment to applied field):
$$\alpha = \frac{\mu}{E} = \frac{q^2/m}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) - i\gamma\omega}$$

**Step 3:** Polarisation P (dipole moment per unit volume, summing N atoms):
$$P = N\alpha\varepsilon_0 E = \varepsilon_0\chi^{(1)} E$$

**Therefore, the linear susceptibility:**

> **[RED BOX]**

$$\chi^{(1)}(\omega) = \frac{Nq^2/m\varepsilon_0}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) - i\gamma\omega}$$

This is a **complex quantity**: the real part relates to refractive index (dispersion), the imaginary part to absorption. The **detuning** (ω₀² − ω²) determines how far from resonance we are.

---

## Physical Behaviour of χ(ω)

Plot χ vs ω:

| Regime | ω ≪ ω₀ | ω = ω₀ | ω ≫ ω₀ |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| Displacement | large, in-phase | maximum (90° lag) | zero (180° lag) |
| Phase | 0° | 90° | 180° |
| Response | DC offset (quasi-static) | maximum susceptibility | no response |

> *Just like pushing someone on a swing: low frequency → small constant displacement; at resonance → maximum amplitude; high frequency → the system can't respond.*

---

## Polarisation Timescales

Different types of polarisation respond on different timescales:

| Type | Particles moved | Timescale | Frequency range | EM range |
|------|----------------|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| **Electronic** | Electrons only | ~10⁻¹⁵ s (femtoseconds) | UV to visible | UV–visible |
| **Atomic (vibrational)** | Atomic nuclei + their electrons | ~10⁻¹² s (picoseconds) | Infrared | Infrared |
| **Orientational** | Whole molecules rotating | ~10⁻⁶ s (microseconds) | Microwave–far-IR | Microwave |
| **Ionic** | Ions in a crystal | ~10⁻³ s (milliseconds) | — | — |

> *Electrons are light → respond fast → electronic polarisation in femtoseconds.*  
> *Atoms are heavy → respond slower → atomic/vibrational polarisation in picoseconds.*

**Implications for chemical contrast:**
- **Electronic polarisation:** Governed by length-scale L of conjugated system. Good for fluorescence/absorption imaging, BUT does not directly tell you about chemistry — just the size of the conjugated system.
- **Atomic polarisation:** The resonant frequency depends on the **specific atoms** and **bond strength** (k), giving a unique molecular fingerprint. This is where chemical specificity comes from.

## Vibrational Spectroscopy (Introduction)

> *"Vibrational spectroscopy is a massive field linking chemistry, physics, and biology. The key is that bonds between atoms vibrate, and the frequency at which they vibrate is unique to the molecule."*

Goal: **probe atomic polarisation** to get chemical information.

Two methods:
1. **Infrared (IR) absorption** — direct absorption of IR photons exciting vibrational transitions
2. **Raman scattering** — inelastic scattering of visible/IR photons from vibrational modes

Selection rules (introduced here, derived in L07):
- **IR active:** requires a **change in dipole moment** during the vibration
- **Raman active:** requires a **change in polarizability** during the vibration

> *These are in red boxes — you need to remember both.*

The combination of IR and Raman gives **complementary information** — some vibrations are IR active but not Raman, and vice versa.

## Setting Up the Atomic Oscillator

The diatomic molecule as two masses on a spring (mass m₁ and m₂, spring constant k):

**Reduced mass:**
$$\mu_r = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}$$

**Resonant frequency:**
$$\omega_{vib} = \sqrt{\frac{k}{\mu_r}}$$

Typical spring constants:
- Single bond: k ≈ 5×10² N/m
- Double bond: k ≈ 2×5×10² N/m (approx. double)
- Triple bond: k ≈ 3×5×10² N/m (approx. triple)

> *Knowing the periodic table masses and these approximate spring constants, you can calculate the expected vibrational frequency for any chemical bond.*
