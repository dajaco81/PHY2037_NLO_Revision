# L04 — Optical Imaging Part 2: Resolution, Confocal Microscopy, and Scattering

**Date:** 20 January 2026 | **Slot:** Tuesday 12:32

---

Today: spatial properties of imaging — how deep can we go, how small can we see, and how we form 3D images. We're building the case for why we need nonlinear optics to overcome the limitations of conventional imaging.

---

## Numerical Aperture (NA)

The ability of an objective lens to resolve fine detail is characterised by the **Numerical Aperture**:

> **[RED BOX]**

$$\text{NA} = n \sin\alpha$$

Where:
- n = refractive index of the medium between lens and sample
- α = half-angle of the maximum cone of light that can enter/exit the objective

**How to increase NA:**
1. Increase α: bring the lens closer (shorter focal length) or use a larger diameter lens
2. Increase n: use immersion oil (n ≈ 1.4–1.5) or water (n ≈ 1.33) instead of air (n = 1)

Microscope objectives are labelled with their NA (and immersion medium).

---

## Airy Disk and Diffraction Limit

When light is focused through a circular aperture, it produces a diffraction pattern — a bright central disk (the **Airy disk**) surrounded by rings. 94% of the light is in the central disk.

**Radius of the Airy disk central maximum:**

$$r = \frac{1.22 \lambda}{D}$$

Where D is the aperture diameter. (The 1.22 comes from Bessel functions — just accept it.)

**Abbe diffraction limit** (minimum resolvable feature size, practical microscopy):

$$d_{lateral} = \frac{\lambda}{2\text{NA}}$$

In the axial direction:

$$d_{axial} = \frac{2\lambda}{\text{NA}^2}$$

**Rayleigh Criterion:** Two point sources are just resolved when the maximum of one diffraction pattern coincides with the minimum of the other.

> **[RED BOX — derivation]** Converting from the angular Rayleigh criterion (θ_min = 1.22λ/D) to a distance-based criterion using NA:

Starting from: the minimum resolvable angle θ = 1.22λ/D, and noting that the large-diameter limit D = 2f·tan(α) ≈ 2f·sin(α) for the lens geometry:

$$d = 1.22\frac{\lambda f}{D} \approx 1.22\frac{\lambda}{2\sin\alpha} = \frac{0.61\lambda}{\text{NA}}$$

*(The factor of 1.61 in the transcript is correct for the exact Rayleigh criterion; Abbe's criterion gives 0.5λ/NA. Both are approximations. The key point is d ∝ λ/NA.)*

> **[EXAM HINT]** The derivation converting the angular Rayleigh criterion to a distance using NA is examinable. Items in **red boxes** will need to be reproduced.

**Gaussian approximation:** The Airy disk Bessel function is approximated by a Gaussian for the central lobe. This gives a much easier function to work with. We'll use this Gaussian PSF throughout.

## Point Spread Function (PSF)

The PSF describes the 3D intensity distribution at the focus of an objective lens. In the lateral plane it looks like the Airy disk; in the axial direction it extends much further (the "hourglass" shape).

$$\text{PSF} \approx \exp\left(-\frac{r^2}{2\sigma_r^2}\right) \exp\left(-\frac{z^2}{2\sigma_z^2}\right)$$

FWHM_lateral ≈ 0.61λ/NA (from Abbe)  
FWHM_axial ≈ 2λ/NA² (from Abbe)

## Confocal Microscopy

**Problem with wide-field fluorescence microscopy:** A thick sample fluoresces throughout the entire hourglass-shaped PSF, not just from the focal plane. Out-of-focus light blurs the image.

**Confocal solution:** Place a **pinhole** at the focus of both:
1. The excitation light (confocal excitation)
2. The detection path (confocal detection)

Both pinholes are at the same focal distance (confocal = same focus). Only light from the focal plane follows the black ray diagram and makes it through the detection pinhole. Out-of-plane fluorescence is **blocked**.

**Result:** Virtual optical sectioning through thick tissue — images look like the sample was physically sliced to a thin section, without actually slicing it.

**[Dichoic mirror]:** A special mirror that passes the excitation wavelength but reflects the emission wavelength. This separates excitation from emission in the same optical path.

### Point Scanning

Confocal detection requires single-point detection (no image formed on a 2D array). Instead:
1. Scan the laser spot across the sample in a raster pattern
2. Record intensity at a detector at each (x, y, z) coordinate
3. Build up a 3D image computationally

**Galvanometer scanner:** A pair of mirrors, one oscillating in x and one in y, steers the laser beam across the sample. This is fast enough for near-video-rate 2D imaging and can be combined with a z-stage for 3D stacks.

## Rayleigh Scattering and the Depth Penetration Problem

> **[RED BOX]**

$$I_{scattered} \propto \frac{1}{\lambda^4}$$

This means:
- **Short wavelengths scatter much more** than long wavelengths
- Doubling the wavelength reduces scattering by a factor of **2⁴ = 16**

**Why this kills confocal microscopy in tissue:**

When the emitted fluorescence photons undergo scattering on the way back out through the tissue, they arrive at the detection pinhole at the wrong angle — **the pinhole rejects them**. To compensate, you have to increase laser power, which risks burning the sample.

**Demo:** Tank of milky water — infrared torch (850 nm) passes through almost unscattered while blue (488 nm) and red (633 nm) are strongly scattered. Visible only on a smartphone camera (infrared-sensitive).

> This demonstrates why working with near-infrared is extremely dangerous — it looks dim to the eye but is actually very bright. Laser safety is critical in the near-infrared.

---

## The Fundamental Tradeoff

| Factor | Favours shorter wavelength | Favours longer wavelength |
|--------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Spatial resolution | ✓ (d ∝ λ) | ✗ |
| Scattering depth penetration | ✗ (I ∝ 1/λ⁴) | ✓ |
| Contrast mechanism (absorption/fluorescence) | Dictated by molecule | Cannot be changed |

If we want to image fluorescence with blue excitation → we're stuck using blue light → high scattering → cannot go deep.

> **To overcome this, we need to decouple the excitation wavelength from the contrast mechanism wavelength. This is exactly what nonlinear optics achieves.**

---

## Summary: Limitations of Conventional Optical Microscopy

1. **Requires fluorescent labels** — not always appropriate (small molecules, in-vivo imaging)
2. **Confocal detection needed** for 3D imaging — but scattering defeats the pinhole
3. **Fundamental wavelength tradeoff** — resolution vs. depth penetration vs. contrast mechanism
4. **Poor depth penetration** in scattering tissue (typically < 100 µm for confocal)
5. **Phototoxicity** — short-wavelength (blue/UV) photons are more damaging to biology

> **All of these limitations can be overcome to varying degrees using nonlinear optical excitation.**
