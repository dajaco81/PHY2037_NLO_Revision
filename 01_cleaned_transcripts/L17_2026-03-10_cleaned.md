## How Lasers Work

**Gain medium:** Material that amplifies light through stimulated emission (population inversion).

**Laser cavity:** Two mirrors facing each other. Photons bounce back and forth, stimulating emission from the gain medium. One mirror is partially transmissive → output beam.

**Cavity modes:** A cavity of length L supports standing wave modes with wavelengths:

$$\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n} \implies \nu_n = \frac{nc}{2L}$$

**Mode spacing:**

$$\Delta\nu_{mode} = \frac{c}{2L}$$

For CW single-mode laser: insert a narrow-band filter inside cavity to select only one mode.

For pulsed laser: want to support **many modes simultaneously** → broad bandwidth needed.

## Mode Locking: Generating Ultrashort Pulses

**Principle:** If all the cavity modes are forced to have the **same phase** (mode-locked), they add constructively at one point in the cavity to form a very short pulse. Between pulses, the different phases cause destructive interference.

The repetition rate = c/2L (time for pulse to travel round trip).

**Challenge:** Normally, modes have random phases → continuous but noisy emission. Must force phase locking.

### Method 1: Saturatable Absorber Mode Locking (SESAM)

**Device:** A semiconductor chip (SESAM = Semiconductor Saturable Absorber Mirror) placed in the cavity.

**How it works:**
- At low intensity: semiconductor absorbs light → high loss → modes don't build up
- At high intensity (when modes happen to add constructively): all electrons in valence band are excited to conduction band → absorber **saturates** → loss drops → the pulse survives

**Relaxation time:** Electrons return to valence band in ~0.5–30 ps (through vibrational thermalization).

> Because relaxation time is ~picoseconds, this method favours **picosecond pulse generation** (matches CARS/SRS requirement).

### Method 2: Kerr Lens Mode Locking (KLM)

**Key physics:** The **Kerr effect** — intensity-dependent refractive index:

$$n = n_0 + n_2 I$$

Where n₂ is the nonlinear refractive index coefficient (a χ⁽³⁾ effect). High-intensity beams experience a higher refractive index in the centre of the beam (Gaussian profile) than at the edges → the material acts like a **focusing lens** for high-intensity light.

**How it works:**
- A hard aperture is placed after the gain medium
- High-intensity pulses are self-focused by the Kerr lens → pass through the aperture
- Low-intensity CW light is not focused → blocked by the aperture

**Advantages:** No physical saturable absorber — the nonlinear medium itself (e.g., Ti:Sapphire crystal) acts as the mode-locking element. Response is essentially instantaneous (Kerr effect responds on <10 fs timescale).

> **[EXAM HINT]** Can be asked to draw and explain either SESAM or KLM, showing the intensity-dependent loss curve and explaining which pulse widths each supports.

## Dispersion Compensation in Femtosecond Cavities

For 100 fs pulses: bandwidth ≈ 4.4/pulse_width_ps ≈ 4.4/0.1 ≈ 44 GHz × 100 ≈ 4.4 THz → ~10 nm bandwidth at 800 nm.

All optical elements in the cavity (gain crystal, lens, mirrors) have normal GVD → stretch the pulses. Must compensate.

**Grating pair / prism pair inside cavity:**

Set up so that longer wavelengths travel a longer optical path (compensating the shorter path they travel through the dispersive elements where they're slower).

> Be able to sketch the geometry showing how the grating pair gives different path lengths to different wavelengths. No calculations needed — just the principle.

## Ti:Sapphire Laser

The most common ultrafast laser for NLO microscopy:
- **Gain medium:** Ti³⁺-doped sapphire crystal; tunable from ~690–1080 nm
- **Mode locking:** KLM using the crystal itself as the Kerr medium
- **Dispersion compensation:** Prism pair or chirped mirrors inside cavity
- **Pumped by:** ~5 W at 532 nm (doubled Nd:YAG or doubled fiber laser)
- **Output:** ~80 MHz repetition rate, ~100 fs pulses, ~700 mW average power (at peak output wavelength)

## Synchronised Two-Colour Excitation

For CARS/SRS, two synchronized pulse trains at different wavelengths are required.

**Challenge:** Two independent lasers have different repetition rates and random phase relationships → pulses never overlap.

**Method 1: Electronic synchronisation (Keltner method)**
- Pick off a small fraction of each laser output onto photodiodes
- Compare the repetition rates electronically (using the 19th harmonic of the rep rate)
- Apply an error signal to a piezo-electric mirror in one cavity to match the cavity lengths
- Achieves ~20 fs jitter — fine for ps pulses, too large for 100 fs pulses

**Method 2: Optical injection locking (our group's development)**
- Take ~10% of laser 1's output and inject it into the cavity of laser 2
- The Kerr lens mechanism then locks laser 2 to laser 1 automatically
- Jitter < measurement threshold → can overlap 100 fs pulses reliably

## Frequency Conversion: SHG and OPO

### Second Harmonic Generation (SHG) for Frequency Conversion

Ultrafast lasers typically output in the near-infrared (700–1100 nm). To get visible wavelengths, send through a periodically-poled (quasi-phase-matched) crystal:

$$\lambda_{SHG} = \frac{\lambda_{fundamental}}{2}$$

1030 nm → 515 nm (green) via SHG of a Yb:fiber laser.

### Optical Parametric Oscillator (OPO)

**For generating a tunable second wavelength for CARS/SRS:**

An OPO uses **difference frequency generation** in a resonant cavity:
- Input: **pump** photon at ω_P
- Output: **signal** at ω_S + **idler** at ω_i
- Energy conservation: ω_P = ω_S + ω_i → photon splitting

**Cavity design:** The signal wavelength resonates in the cavity (mirrors reflect ω_S). Each pump pulse traverses the nonlinear crystal, generating signal photons that build up over many round trips.

**Wavelength calculation:**

> **[RED BOX]**

$$\frac{1}{\lambda_P} = \frac{1}{\lambda_S} + \frac{1}{\lambda_i}$$

**Temperature tuning:** The crystal temperature controls the phase matching condition → tunes the output wavelengths.

**For CARS:** Combine 1030 nm pump → SHG gives 515 nm → 515 nm seeds OPO to give tunable Stokes wavelength in 700–1000 nm range. The pump and Stokes pulses are automatically synchronized (same origin laser).
