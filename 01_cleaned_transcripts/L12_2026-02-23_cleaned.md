## Why We Need Pulsed Lasers

For nonlinear optical processes, the material must be driven with an electric field approaching the **atomic field strength**:

$$E_{at} \approx 5\times10^{11} \text{ V m}^{-1}$$

Converting to intensity using I = ½cε₀E²:

$$I_{required} \approx \frac{1}{2}\times 3\times10^8 \times 8.85\times10^{-12} \times (5\times10^{11})^2 \approx 3\times10^{20} \text{ W m}^{-2}$$

This is an **enormous** intensity.

**Strategy 1: Focus spatially.** Focusing down to the diffraction limit for green light (~500 nm spot):

$$A = \pi r^2 \approx \pi\times(250\times10^{-9})^2 \approx 2\times10^{-13} \text{ m}^2$$

Required power: P = I × A ≈ 3×10²⁰ × 2×10⁻¹³ ≈ **6×10⁷ W = 60 MW**

Even at the diffraction limit, continuous-wave (CW) laser would need ~60 MW average power — enough to burn through the sample and the lab.

**Strategy 2: Focus temporally.** Instead of increasing average power, deliver the same total energy in very short bursts. Peak intensity can be extremely high, but the average power remains manageable.

> *"It's like the difference between a machine gun (many light bullets rapidly) and a tank (one heavy shell slowly). The total energy is the same, but the peak force is very different."*

Analogy: High-heeled shoes vs. flat shoes — same weight but dramatically different pressure.

## Pulse Characterisation Parameters

> **[RED BOX]** Must remember these equations:

**Pulse energy:**
$$E_{pulse} = \frac{P_{av}}{f_{rep}}$$

Where P_av = average power (W), f_rep = repetition rate (pulses/s = Hz).

**Peak power:**
$$P_{peak} = \frac{E_{pulse}}{\tau_p}$$

Where τ_p = pulse duration (FWHM of intensity pulse).

**Peak intensity:**
$$I_{peak} = \frac{P_{peak}}{A}$$

Where A = beam area at focus (m²).

> *"These are in a red box — you need to remember them. But actually, you can derive them from first principles: energy per pulse × pulses per second = average power."*

## Example: 80 MHz vs. 1 kHz Laser Comparison

Both lasers: P_av = 10 mW, τ_p = 100 fs.

**80 MHz laser** (typ. oscillator for NLO microscopy):
$$E_{pulse} = \frac{10\times10^{-3}}{80\times10^6} = 1.25 \times 10^{-10} \text{ J} = 0.125 \text{ nJ}$$

$$P_{peak} = \frac{1.25\times10^{-10}}{100\times10^{-15}} = 1.25 \text{ kW}$$

**1 kHz laser** (typ. amplified system):
$$E_{pulse} = \frac{10\times10^{-3}}{10^3} = 10^{-5} \text{ J} = 10 \text{ µJ}$$

$$P_{peak} = \frac{10^{-5}}{100\times10^{-15}} = 10^8 \text{ W} = 100 \text{ MW}$$

> Same average power (10 mW), same pulse width (100 fs), but the 1 kHz system has **10⁸ × higher peak power** than the 80 MHz system. This is the tank vs. machine gun analogy.

## Temporal Pulse Separation

The separation between pulses (in metres) = speed of light × time between pulses:

$$d = \frac{c}{f_{rep}}$$

- 80 MHz: d = 3×10⁸ / 8×10⁷ ≈ **3.75 m** between pulses
- 1 kHz: d = 3×10⁸ / 10³ = **300 km** between pulses

**Application:** For experiments using two synchronized pulse trains (e.g., CARS), you need to temporally overlap the pulses. You use a **delay stage** — a movable mirror that changes the optical path length. For 80 MHz pulses (3.75 m separation), the delay stage can overlap pulses with sub-femtosecond precision using just millimetre-scale mirror movement.

## Pulse Bandwidth (Uncertainty Principle Estimate)

Squeezing light into short pulses → uncertainty in timing Δt is small → uncertainty in energy ΔE is large:

$$\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

$$\Delta\nu \cdot \Delta t \geq \frac{1}{4\pi}$$

**Example:** 1 ps pulse → Δν ≈ 1/(4π × 10⁻¹²) ≈ 80 GHz = 500 GHz (depending on exact formula used — the point is the order of magnitude).

> **[IMPORTANT — COMMON MISTAKE]** Converting Δν to Δλ:

Since f = c/λ, differentiating:

$$|\Delta\lambda| = \frac{\lambda^2}{c} |\Delta\nu|$$

This is **NOT** a linear conversion. Δλ depends on **λ²** — so the same bandwidth in frequency corresponds to very different bandwidth in wavelength depending on where in the spectrum you are.

**Example comparison:** 1 ps pulses at 800 nm vs. 1600 nm

At 800 nm: Δλ ≈ (800×10⁻⁹)² / (3×10⁸) × Δν ≈ 1 nm  
At 1600 nm: Δλ ≈ (1600×10⁻⁹)² / (3×10⁸) × Δν ≈ 4 nm

> *Same Δν but 4× wider bandwidth in wavelength at 1600 nm than 800 nm!*

## Chirped Pulses and Dispersion

The electric field of a pulse can be written as:

$$E(t) = \sqrt{I(t)} \cdot e^{i\phi(t)}$$

Where φ(t) is the **time-dependent phase** — it describes how the instantaneous frequency changes within the pulse.

**Chirp:** When φ(t) is not constant, the frequency changes across the pulse — the pulse is "chirped". Blue components lead or lag red components.

**Physical cause: Group Velocity Dispersion (GVD)**

In a dispersive medium (e.g., glass), n increases with ω (normal dispersion). Blue light travels slower than red light. A short pulse sent through glass:
- Red components arrive **first** (experience lower n → faster)
- Blue components arrive **later** (experience higher n → slower)
- Pulse is **stretched** (dispersed/chirped)

This is exactly analogous to thunder: a lightning bolt produces a very short impulsive sound, but by the time it reaches you through the atmosphere, dispersion has stretched it into the familiar rumble.

> **[EXAM HINT]** Be able to explain why pulses get stretched by dispersion and how the chirp arises. Be able to state which colour arrives first (red = normal dispersion → red faster).
