## Recap: Coupled Wave Equations

Recap of the derivation from L10:

Starting from the nonlinear wave equation:

$$\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} - \frac{n^2}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} = \frac{1}{\varepsilon_0 c^2}\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{P}_{NL}}{\partial t^2}$$

For SFG (ω₃ = ω₁ + ω₂), with fields written as slowly-varying amplitudes times rapidly-oscillating carriers:

$$E_j = A_j(z) e^{i(k_j z - \omega_j t)} + \text{c.c.}$$

Substituting and applying SVEA (∂²A/∂z² ≈ 0):

> **[RED BOX]**

$$\frac{dA_3}{dz} = \frac{i\omega_3 d_{eff}}{n_3 c} A_1 A_2 e^{i\Delta k z}$$

$$\frac{dA_1}{dz} = \frac{i\omega_1 d_{eff}}{n_1 c} A_3 A_2^* e^{-i\Delta k z}$$

$$\frac{dA_2}{dz} = \frac{i\omega_2 d_{eff}}{n_2 c} A_3 A_1^* e^{-i\Delta k z}$$

**For Δk = 0 (perfect phase matching):** The exponential goes to 1, and integrating gives amplitude increasing linearly with z → **intensity grows as z²**.

## Why Normal Dispersion Prevents Perfect Phase Matching

For SHG (ω₃ = 2ω₁, so ω₁ = ω₂):

$$k_3 - 2k_1 = \frac{n_3 \cdot 2\omega}{c} - 2\frac{n_1 \omega}{c} = \frac{2\omega}{c}(n_3 - n_1) = \Delta k$$

For Δk = 0 we need n(2ω) = n(ω).

But in materials with **normal dispersion** (n increases with ω), we always have n(2ω) > n(ω) → Δk > 0 → no perfect phase matching.

For SFG, the condition n₃ω₃ = n₁ω₁ + n₂ω₂ requires:

$$n_3 > n_1 \text{ and } n_3 > n_2$$

Which is impossible in normal dispersion (higher frequency has higher n, but the constraint can't be satisfied).

> **All lossless materials have normal dispersion → Δk ≠ 0 always.** We need tricks to overcome this.

## Phase Matching Scheme 1: Angle Tuning (Birefringence)

> *(Green box — need to understand concept but not equations)*

**Birefringent materials** have two different refractive indices depending on the polarisation direction:
- **Ordinary ray** (o-ray): polarisation perpendicular to the optic axis
- **Extraordinary ray** (e-ray): polarisation parallel to the optic axis; n depends on propagation angle θ

By rotating the crystal (changing θ), you can tune the extraordinary refractive index experienced by one of the beams, bringing the phase mismatch Δk closer to zero.

**Limitation — walkoff:** At angles other than 0° or 90°, the energy flow (Poynting vector) is not parallel to the phase velocity (k-vector). This causes the beams to physically separate as they propagate through the crystal — reducing the effective interaction length.

---

## Phase Matching Scheme 2: Temperature Tuning

> *(Green box)*

Some birefringent materials (especially **lithium niobate**, LiNbO₃) have strongly temperature-dependent birefringence. At 90° phase matching (no walkoff), heat the crystal to ~140°C to tune the refractive index difference.

Benefits: no walkoff, tunable wavelength conversion.

---

## Phase Matching Scheme 3: Quasi-Phase Matching (Periodic Poling)

> *(Important — understand the principle)*

**Periodic poling** (e.g., **PPLN** = periodically-poled lithium niobate):

The crystal is fabricated as alternating domains where the crystal orientation (and therefore the sign of χ⁽²⁾) is flipped.

**How it works:**

Without phase matching, the signal oscillates up and down as Δk builds up. By choosing the domain period such that:

$$\Lambda_{period} = \frac{2\pi}{\Delta k} = \frac{\pi}{\Delta k/2}$$

When the signal would start to decrease (at the coherence length π/Δk), the crystal orientation flips, reversing the sign of the nonlinear coupling. This effectively resets the phase mismatch, allowing the signal to continue growing.

**Result:** A stepped increase in signal amplitude — not as efficient as perfect phase matching, but can be engineered for any target wavelength.

> The manufacturer designs the domain period for your specific wavelength combination.

## Phase Matching in a Tight Focus (Microscopy)

When focusing a beam through a high-NA microscope objective:
- The beam contains a **range of k-vector directions** (all the angles within the cone of the focus)
- This relaxes the strict collinear phase matching condition (a range of Δk values can partially cancel)
- Phase matching becomes less of a constraint

**Gouy Phase Shift:**

A light beam passing through a tight focus (waist) acquires an additional phase shift of **π radians** going from one side of the focus to the other. Named after Louis-Georges Gouy.

> **[RED BOX — concept]**

**Physical origin:** The beam is converging before the focus and diverging after, and the Gaussian beam has a slightly different phase velocity compared to a plane wave.

**Implication for NLO in a microscope:**

The Gouy phase shift contributes to the effective Δk:
- For SHG (χ⁽²⁾ process): each input photon contributes π/2 to the Gouy phase → total π shift. Combined with the normal Δk from dispersion, the effective interaction length must satisfy L < λ₁/2. This is *just achievable* with a typical tight focus.
- For THG (χ⁽³⁾ process): three input photons → total 3π/2 Gouy phase shift → interaction length must be L < λ₁/3. This is harder to satisfy.

**Consequence for THG:** THG in a tight focus only generates signal from **very thin objects or interfaces** — where the interaction length is shorter than λ/3. This gives interface-selective contrast.

---

## Application: Signal Generation in an Imaging Geometry

The phase matching condition I₃ ∝ L² sinc²(ΔkL/2) applies along the z-axis of the microscope. Since L here is the **confocal parameter** (depth of focus), reducing the focal volume:
1. Improves spatial resolution
2. Relaxes the phase matching condition (reduces Δk × L)

This is why nonlinear optical microscopy works better at higher numerical aperture — the tight focus simultaneously provides high intensity (for NLO) and improves phase matching (through smaller interaction length and range of k-vectors).

---

## Summary: Phase Matching Methods

| Method | Principle | Limitation |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Angle tuning | Use birefringence, rotate crystal | Walkoff |
| Temperature tuning | T-dependent birefringence | Limited range |
| Quasi-phase matching | Periodic domain reversal | Needs custom crystal |
| Tight focus (microscopy) | Range of k-vectors in focus; shorter L | Only works for microscopy geometries |
