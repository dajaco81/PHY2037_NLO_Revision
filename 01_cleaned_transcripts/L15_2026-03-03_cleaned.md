## The Nonlinear Squeeze Effect on Spatial Resolution

When using 2PEF (or higher), the signal scales as I². The focal intensity profile approximated by a Gaussian:

$$I(r) \propto e^{-r^2/\sigma^2}$$

For linear fluorescence: PSF_1P ∝ I  
For two-photon: PSF_2P ∝ I² ∝ e^{-2r²/σ²}

This squared Gaussian is equivalent to a Gaussian with σ → σ/√2, giving a narrower PSF.

> **[RED BOX — derivation]**

**FWHM of the n-photon PSF:**

$$\text{FWHM}_{nP} = \frac{\text{FWHM}_{1P}}{\sqrt{n}}$$

More precisely: for 2PEF, the resolution is **1/√2 ≈ 0.707 times** the single-photon diffraction limit (better resolution, not worse, despite using 2× wavelength).

### Example Calculation

Fluorescent dye with absorption maximum at 400 nm, NA = 1.2:

**1PEF (linear excitation at 400 nm):**
$$d_{1P} = \frac{0.61\lambda}{\text{NA}} = \frac{0.61\times400}{1.2} = 203 \text{ nm}$$

**2PEF (excitation at 800 nm):**

Without the squeeze effect: d_2P = 0.61×800/1.2 = 407 nm (worse by factor 2)

With the nonlinear squeeze: d_2P = 407/√2 ≈ **288 nm**

**3PEF (excitation at 1200 nm):**

Without: d_3P = 0.61×1200/1.2 = 610 nm

With squeeze: d_3P = 610/√3 ≈ **352 nm**

> *In scattering tissue, 1PEF can't achieve 203 nm beyond ~50 µm depth. 2PEF achieves 288 nm to ~500 µm depth. The absolute resolution is worse, but the practical achievable resolution at depth is far better.*

## Parametric vs. Non-Parametric Processes

> **[RED BOX]** Know the difference — it determines whether phase matching matters.

| Type | Definition | Examples | Phase matching needed? |
|------|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| **Parametric** | Quantum state of material **unchanged** — no net energy deposited in material | SHG, SFG, DFG, THG, four-wave mixing | **Yes** — coherence maintained; destructive interference if not matched |
| **Non-parametric** | Quantum state changes — energy deposited in material | Fluorescence, two-photon absorption, Raman (spontaneous) | **No** — coherence lost through vibrational relaxation |

**Physical reason:** In parametric processes, photons interact and immediately re-emit without disturbing the material's quantum state. All emitted photons are coherent with the input. If they're out of phase, they cancel.

In non-parametric processes, the material absorbs energy (excites a vibrational state), waits a few picoseconds, then emits. The coherence is lost. Signals add **incoherently** (intensities add, not amplitudes).

## Second Harmonic Generation (SHG) in a Microscope

SHG is a **second-order parametric process**: 2ω₁ → ω₂ = 2ω₁.

**Phase matching requirement in tight focus:**

The Gouy phase shift contributes a phase of π/2 per input beam (2 beams for SHG → total Gouy contribution = π). Combined with material dispersion Δk, the condition for the signal not to cancel is:

$$L < \frac{\pi}{\Delta k + k_{Gouy}} \approx \frac{\lambda_1}{2}$$

For typical visible NLO experiments (e.g., 800 nm → 400 nm), L ≈ λ₁/2 is achievable with a tightly focused beam in a standard high-NA objective. **SHG works in a microscope.**

**Selection rule:** Only from **non-centrosymmetric** structures (χ⁽²⁾ ≠ 0).

**Biologically relevant SHG-active molecules:**

1. **Collagen** — the most abundant structural protein, found in connective tissue, cartilage, skin, extracellular matrix. Non-centrosymmetric due to its triple-helix structure.
2. **Myosin** — found in muscle fibers (thick filaments). Ordered arrangement gives non-centrosymmetry.

**SHG images collagen and myosin for free** — no labelling required.

**Applications:**
- Imaging cartilage degradation in arthritis
- Imaging collagen remodelling in tumour microenvironment
- Imaging muscle fibre structure in muscular dystrophy research

## Third Harmonic Generation (THG) in a Microscope

THG: 3ω₁ → ω₂ = 3ω₁ (third-order process).

**Phase matching in tight focus:**

For THG, Gouy phase from 3 input beams → 3π/2 total Gouy phase. Combined with larger dispersion Δk (factor 3 difference in wavelength), the condition is:

$$L < \frac{\lambda_1}{3}$$

This is very difficult to satisfy — only very thin objects (sub-wavelength thin) generate THG.

**Result:** THG is **interface-selective** — you only get signal from:
- Interfaces between materials of different refractive index
- Objects thinner than ~λ/3

> *"This gives you something like phase contrast microscopy, but in 3D."*

**Applications:** Imaging thin structures, lipid vesicles, cell membranes.

## Four-Wave Mixing (4WM) and CARS Preview

**Process:** 2ω₁ − ω₂ = ω₃ (two pump photons at ω₁, one Stokes photon at ω₂, signal at ω₃)

**Key property:** For this specific combination, the Gouy phase shifts **cancel out**:
- 2 pump photons contribute +2 × (π/2) = +π phase
- 1 Stokes photon contributes −(π/2) phase (negative frequency component)
- Net Gouy phase = +π − (π/2) = +π/2, which partially cancels the dispersion Δk

This means **4WM can generate signal from bulk material** in a tight focus, not just from interfaces.

**Jablonski diagram comparison:**

If the 2ω₁ − ω₂ energy difference matches:
- An **electronic transition** → electronic resonance → strong signal regardless of molecule (graphene example)
- A **vibrational transition** (ω₁ − ω₂ = ω_vib) → **this looks exactly like a Raman transition on the Jablonski diagram!**

> *"This is where it gets really cool — next lecture."*

The combination (ω_1 − ω_2) tuned to match a Raman-active vibrational mode gives **Coherent Anti-Stokes Raman Scattering (CARS)** — the topic of L16.
