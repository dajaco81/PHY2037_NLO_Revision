## Introduction to the Topic: What Is Optical Imaging?

Obviously we take it for granted that we see the world around us by looking at images. Whether it's a screen, microscopy, astronomy, or photography, the aim is to understand the **spatial organisation** of materials at different scales.

**Key imaging specifications:**
- **Depth penetration** — how deep can you go into an object?
- **Spatial resolution** — the smallest separation between two objects that can be distinguished. (Not the same as magnification.) *Do you all remember the Rayleigh criterion from first year?*
- **Sensitivity** — the lowest number of molecules / dimmest star detectable
- **Specificity** — ability to tell different molecule types apart
- **Contrast** — the mechanism that makes different parts of the image look different

### Contrast mechanisms illustrated by a landscape photo:
- **Absorption** — the camera frame is black because it absorbs all wavelengths; the grass is green because chlorophyll absorbs red and blue, reflecting green
- **Scattering** — the sky is orange/blue due to **Rayleigh scattering** (I ∝ 1/λ⁴): blue light scattered more; at sunset you see the unscattered red light
- **Reflection** — the pond looks like the sky (specular reflection)

> The sky is blue. The sunset is red. *Keep coming back to this: scattering I ∝ 1/λ⁴ explains why longer wavelengths penetrate deeper into scattering media.*

### Imaging beyond the visible:
- Infrared cameras for heat maps
- UV light to excite fluorescence in cells
- The contrast mechanism and contrast specificity depend on which part of the EM spectrum you probe

Most of what we'll look at is **under the microscope** — because you need a very tight focus to do nonlinear optics, and that's why NLO lends itself to microscopy applications.

## The Fundamental Equation

> **[RED BOX]** This is the key equation that underpins everything in this course:

$$\mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0 \chi \mathbf{E}$$

- **P** = induced polarisation (dipole moment per unit volume; units: C m⁻²)
- **E** = applied electric field (the oscillating field from the laser/light)
- **χ** (chi) = **electric susceptibility** — the magic property of the material that contains all information about how it responds
- In isotropic materials χ is a scalar; in general it is a **tensor** (a matrix that maps the vector E onto the vector P)

**Physical picture:** A fixed positive charge surrounded by a cloud of electrons. Apply an electric field → electrons get displaced → creates an induced dipole → each little dipole emits its own radiation.

χ is not constant: it depends on **frequency** (wavelength of the driving field). At some frequencies it's easy to drag electrons away from their atoms; at others it isn't. This is the origin of dispersion and selective absorption.

### The Power Series (nonlinear extension):

$$\mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0 \left[ \chi^{(1)} \mathbf{E} + \chi^{(2)} \mathbf{E}^2 + \chi^{(3)} \mathbf{E}^3 + \cdots \right]$$

The **real key** behind nonlinear optics: this power series means higher-order terms respond to E², E³, etc., and produce polarisations (and hence new radiation) at frequencies different from the driving field.

## Brief History of Nonlinear Optics

Nonlinear optics is ~65 years old (a baby compared to classical thermodynamics).

**1960:** First laser demonstrated by Maiman — ruby crystal pumped by photographic flash lamp, first stimulated light emission. Huge discovery.

**1961 (one year later!):** Franken demonstrated **Second Harmonic Generation (SHG)** — first nonlinear optical process ever observed.
- Took a ruby laser (694.3 nm, red), focused it onto a quartz crystal
- Detected a tiny spot of blue light at 347 nm — half the wavelength, twice the frequency
- The signal was so tiny that the peer reviewer initially thought it was a speck of rubbish on the photograph and removed it
- Demonstrates: two red photons in → one blue photon out (energy conservation via photon energy E = hf)
- This is second harmonic generation, arising from the χ² term in the power series

> **[EXAM HINT]** Remember the year: **1961** — one year after the first laser.

**1980s:** Donna Strickland (Nobel Prize) developed **chirped pulse amplification**, allowing very high intensity ultrafast laser pulses. This made practical nonlinear optics achievable.

## Why We Need Lasers

None of this would be possible without lasers. Key properties:
- **Well-defined wavelength** and narrow bandwidth
- **Very high intensities**
- **Spatially and temporally coherent** — all photons oscillating in phase both in space and time. This allows focusing to very small spots and pulsed operation.
- **Time resolution** — can produce very short pulses (femtoseconds)

> It was the invention of the laser that kicked off the practical field of nonlinear optics.

---

## Why Nonlinear Optics?

Everything is on this list:
1. See things **invisible** with conventional linear contrast mechanisms
2. Excite light–matter interactions that wouldn't respond at the chosen wavelength (e.g., excite UV transitions with IR light)
3. **Enhanced depth penetration** — scattering I ∝ 1/λ⁴, so longer wavelength (IR) excitation scatters much less, but the contrast mechanism (e.g., fluorescence) requires UV/visible absorption. Nonlinear optics decouples the excitation wavelength from the contrast wavelength.
4. **Enhanced interaction efficiency**

> Short wavelength → better resolution but more scattering. Long wavelength → less scattering but worse resolution. **Nonlinear optics allows us to get the best of both worlds.**
