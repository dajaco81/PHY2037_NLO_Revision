## Recap: Key Advantages of Nonlinear Optical Microscopy

> **[RED BOX]** Be able to explain all three points — this is almost certainly an exam question.

1. **No pinhole needed** — nonlinear signal is only generated at the focal spot (I² or I³ dependence confines signal generation)
2. **Deeper penetration** — excitation at 2× or 3× wavelength → scattering reduced by 2⁴=16 or 3⁴=81 fold; emitted signal can scatter freely (detected by large-area detector)
3. **Resolution partially recovered** — 1/√n factor improvement from nonlinear squeeze effect

Tradeoff recap:

| Limitation | How NLO overcomes it |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Fluorescent labels needed | SHG, CARS, SRS give label-free chemical contrast |
| Confocal pinhole blocked by scattering | No pinhole needed (intrinsic confinement) |
| Blue light scatters too much | IR excitation decouples excitation from contrast mechanism |
| Resolution sacrificed by IR | Nonlinear squeeze partially recovers resolution |

## Forward vs. Backward CARS Detection

**Forward CARS:** Signal detected in the same direction as the excitation beam.
- Phase matching for bulk material (L >> π/Δk): signal adds constructively from large volumes → strong signal from any material
- Less specific to small objects

**Backward (EPI) CARS:** Signal detected in the backscattered direction.
- K₃ now points backwards → Δk is now **very large** (nearly 2k₁ instead of near-zero)
- Phase matching condition: signal only from objects where L < π/Δk ≈ λ/2 (sub-wavelength objects)
- **Only generates backwards CARS from interfaces and sub-wavelength particles**

> **[RED BOX]** This is directly applicable to nanoparticle imaging:

In **forward CARS**: bulk tissue gives strong background, nanoparticles hard to see.  
In **backward CARS**: bulk tissue signal is suppressed (destructive interference), **sub-wavelength particles give bright signal**.

**This is a practical application of the phase matching derivation from L10–L11.**

## Nanoparticle Drug Delivery Imaging

**Context:** 95% of new drugs developed fail clinical trials — often due to:
- Toxicity before reaching the target site
- Drug consumed/metabolised before reaching target
- Insolubility

**Nanoparticle encapsulation** (pioneered by Prof. Uma Patel, now Dame): encapsulate drug in nanoparticles (~100–200 nm) to:
- Protect drug from body's defence mechanisms
- Improve solubility
- Control release rate
- Target delivery to specific cells

**Imaging:** Use backward CARS to image nanoparticles in tissues. Can demonstrate that particles are taken up into cells.

**Example:** Colon cancer / gut drug delivery — backward CARS (green: SRS from cells) + SHG (blue: collagen in extracellular matrix) + EPI-CARS (red: drug delivery nanoparticles). Shows nanoparticles clustering near gut wall, then being taken up into cells.

## Virtual H&E with SRS + AI

**Current clinical workflow for cancer diagnosis:**

1. Biopsy → fix in paraffin wax → section (thin slice) → H&E stain → pathologist reads slide

Problems: hours/days delay, inconclusive results common, invasive, can't image large areas.

**NLO approach:** Use SRS microscopy to image unstained tissue. Generate images that look like H&E using:
1. SRS signal at CH₂ stretch → maps lipid content → "eosin-like" pink
2. SRS signal at amide I band → maps protein content → "haematoxylin-like" purple
3. Combine → **virtual H&E image** without any staining

**AI/machine learning:** 

Historical pathology slides are stored by hospitals (thousands from every biopsy for decades). Train neural networks on these images + associated patient outcomes → system learns to predict cancer grade and treatment response from morphological features that humans cannot reliably spot.

Apply trained network to virtual H&E images → real-time cancer diagnosis in the clinic, potentially during endoscopy with an optical fibre.

> *"This would replace the biopsy-wait-results cycle. Surgeons could image large areas during endoscopy in real time, without removing tissue."*

## Deuterium Labelling (Silent Region Imaging)

**Problem:** Small drug molecules may not have a unique Raman peak. Alternatively, you want a "label" that minimally perturbs the molecule.

**Solution:** Replace hydrogen (H) with **deuterium (D)** in the molecule.

**Why this works:**
- C–H stretch: ~2950 cm⁻¹ (above the biological fingerprint region)
- C–D stretch: ~2100 cm⁻¹ (in the "Raman silent" region — 1900–2800 cm⁻¹ where no biological molecules absorb)

From the vibrational frequency formula: ω_vib ∝ 1/√μ_r. Deuterium has twice the mass of hydrogen → reduces the reduced mass → lowers the frequency.

**Chemical equivalence:** C–H and C–D bonds are chemically nearly identical (same electron configuration). The molecule behaves essentially the same as the undeuterated version. This is the **minimal perturbation label** — like replacing the transit van with a tiny GPS tag.

**Application:** Image drug diffusion through skin — see simultaneously: skin lipids (C–H peak), drug molecule (labelled C–D peak), solvent carrier (separately labelled).

### Case Study: Ketoprofen in Skin

- Ketoprofen (anti-inflammatory, like ibuprofen) delivered through skin in a cream
- Imaged using SRS at C–H (skin lipids), drug's natural peak, and deuterated propylene glycol solvent
- Discovery: propylene glycol diffuses faster than ketoprofen → drug crystallises at skin surface → doesn't penetrate deep

**Conclusion for industry:** Use a different solvent that matches the diffusion rate of the drug.

## Microplastics Imaging

**Application:** Detect microplastic particles in marine organisms using EPI-CARS / SRS.

> *"These results were presented to Parliament and helped get microplastics banned from cosmetics."*

The same EPI-CARS / backward phase matching technique that allows detection of drug delivery nanoparticles works for any sub-wavelength material mismatch — including plastic particles in biological tissue.

## SHG Applications in Disease Models

**Collagen imaging:**
- SHG from collagen fibres in connective tissue gives intrinsic (label-free) 3D contrast
- Used to study: arthritis (collagen degradation in cartilage), tumour microenvironment (collagen remodelling), fibrosis

**Myosin imaging:**
- SHG from myosin in muscle fibres
- Used to study: muscular dystrophy, cardiac muscle disease
