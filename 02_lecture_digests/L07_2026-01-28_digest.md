# L07 Digest — Vibrational Spectroscopy: IR and Raman

## Core Concepts

1. **IR absorption:** quantised absorption of IR photons by molecular vibrations. Requires a change in dipole moment.

2. **Raman scattering:** inelastic scattering of visible light. Photon is shifted in frequency by the vibrational frequency. Requires a change in polarizability.

3. **Both are chemically specific** (contrast to electronic polarisation) because the resonant frequency depends on the specific atoms and bond type.

4. **IR limitations:** poor spatial resolution (infrared λ → large d), water absorption, glass incompatible, thermal background.

5. **Raman advantages over IR:** uses visible light → high spatial resolution, glass optics, 3D confocal imaging possible. Disadvantage: very weak (×10⁶ weaker than fluorescence).

6. **Wavenumber units (cm⁻¹):** $\tilde{\nu}$ = 1/λ (in cm). Converts to frequency as $\nu = c\tilde{\nu}$.

## Key Equations

| Equation | Name |
|----------|------|
| ν_vib = (1/2π)√(k/μ_r) | Vibrational frequency |
| μ_r = m₁m₂/(m₁+m₂) | Reduced mass |
| $\tilde{\nu}$ = 1/λ (in cm) | Wavenumber |
| $\mu_{induced} = \alpha_0 E + \frac{1}{2}(dα/dQ)Q_0[\cos(ω_L−ω_{vib})t + \cos(ω_L+ω_{vib})t]$ | Raman modulation (induced dipole) |
| $\lambda_{Stokes} = 1/(1/\lambda_P − \tilde{\nu}_{vib})$ | Stokes wavelength calculation |

## Derivations

### Raman Scattering (Sideband Modulation)

1. Incident field: E = E₀cos(ω_L t)
2. Polarizability oscillates with bond: α = α₀ + (dα/dQ)Q₀cos(ω_vib t)
3. Induced dipole: μ = αE — substitute both time-varying quantities
4. Using cos A·cos B = ½[cos(A−B) + cos(A+B)]:

$$\mu = \alpha_0 E_0\cos(\omega_L t) + \frac{E_0 Q_0}{2}\frac{d\alpha}{dQ}\left[\cos((\omega_L-\omega_{vib})t) + \cos((\omega_L+\omega_{vib})t)\right]$$

Three components: Rayleigh (ω_L), Stokes (ω_L − ω_vib), Anti-Stokes (ω_L + ω_vib)

### Selection Rules (RED BOX — must memorise both)

| Spectroscopy | Selection rule |
|-------------|--------------|
| **IR absorption** | Change in **dipole moment** (dμ/dQ ≠ 0) |
| **Raman scattering** | Change in **polarizability** (dα/dQ ≠ 0) |

### C=O Vibrational Frequency (Example)

- m_C = 12 u, m_O = 16 u
- μ_r = (12×16)/(12+16) × 1.66×10⁻²⁷ kg = 1.14×10⁻²⁶ kg
- k_{C=O} ≈ 1000 N/m (double bond)
- ν_vib = (1/2π)√(1000/1.14×10⁻²⁶) ≈ 4.7×10¹³ Hz
- $\tilde{\nu}$ ≈ 1570 cm⁻¹

## Stokes vs Anti-Stokes

| Feature | Stokes | Anti-Stokes |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Shift | Red (lower energy out) | Blue (higher energy out) |
| Intensity | Stronger (molecule starts in ground state) | Weaker (requires thermally populated vibrational state) |
| Convention | Positive $\tilde{\nu}$ shift | Negative $\tilde{\nu}$ shift |

## Physical Intuition

**IR absorption:** A vibrating molecule whose bond has an oscillating dipole moment acts like a small antenna. It can couple directly to the oscillating electric field of an IR photon and absorb it. Symmetric bonds (e.g., H₂, N₂) have no dipole → IR inactive.

**Raman:** The incoming photon polarises the electron cloud. As the bond vibrates, the polarizability changes, amplitude-modulating the scattered light — like frequency modulation of a radio signal. The sideband frequencies contain the vibrational information.

## Common Mistakes

1. Confusing IR and Raman selection rules (they're different!)
2. Unit mismatch: wavenumber in cm⁻¹ but wavelength in nm or m
3. Thinking anti-Stokes is stronger than Stokes (it's weaker at room temperature)
4. Forgetting that the reduced mass is needed, not just one of the masses
