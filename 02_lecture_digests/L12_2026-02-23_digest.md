# L12 Digest — Laser Pulses: Characterisation and Bandwidth

## Core Concepts

1. **Why pulsed lasers?** Need I ≈ 3×10²⁰ W/m² to approach atomic field strength. Spatial focusing alone (diffraction limit) requires ~75 MW CW — impractical. Temporal squeezing keeps average power low while achieving extreme peak intensities.

2. **Nonlinear signal scales with peak intensity, not average power.** So pulsed operation directly enhances NLO signal even at the same average power.

3. **Three pulse characterisation parameters (RED BOX):**

$$E_{pulse} = \frac{P_{av}}{f_{rep}}$$

$$P_{peak} = \frac{E_{pulse}}{\tau_p}$$

$$I_{peak} = \frac{P_{peak}}{A}$$

4. **Bandwidth ↔ pulse width** (uncertainty principle estimate, then exact via Fourier transform):
   - Short Δt → large Δν (and Δλ)
   - Conversion: **Δλ = (λ²/c)Δν** (not a linear conversion)

5. **Chirped pulses:** Dispersive materials stretch pulses because different λ travel at different speeds. Red arrives before blue in normal dispersion.

## Worked Examples

### 80 MHz vs. 1 kHz comparison (P_av = 10 mW, τ_p = 100 fs)

| Parameter | 80 MHz | 1 kHz |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| E_pulse | 0.125 nJ | 10 µJ |
| P_peak | **1.25 kW** | **100 MW** |
| Pulse separation | 3.75 m | 300 km |

> Same average power, same pulse width; the 1 kHz has 10⁸× higher peak power and is better for single-shot experiments; 80 MHz is better for rapid scanning and temporal overlap.

### Bandwidth conversion (1 ps pulse at 800 nm vs 1600 nm)

$$\Delta\nu \approx \frac{0.441}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.441}{10^{-12}} \approx 441 \text{ GHz}$$

At 800 nm: $\Delta\lambda = \frac{(800\times10^{-9})^2}{3\times10^8}\times441\times10^9 \approx 0.94$ nm

At 1600 nm: $\Delta\lambda = \frac{(1600\times10^{-9})^2}{3\times10^8}\times441\times10^9 \approx 3.8$ nm

→ Four times wider bandwidth required at 1600 nm to achieve the same pulse duration.

## Physical Intuition

**The heel analogy:** Same weight distributed over a small area (stiletto heel) → enormous pressure. Same laser energy delivered over a very short time → enormous peak intensity. The average energy (area under the pulse × rep rate) stays the same.

**Why longer wavelength needs wider bandwidth:** The Stokes shift Δλ = (λ²/c)Δν is proportional to λ². For the same frequency spread (set by the pulse physics), longer λ → wider spread in nm.

## Common Mistakes

1. Using Δλ ≈ (λ/c)Δν (forgetting the λ² — off by a factor of λ/c which is huge!)
2. Mixing up P_av and P_peak in NLO signal calculations
3. Forgetting to divide E_pulse by τ_p (pulse duration not rep rate) to get peak power
4. Using FWHM of amplitude instead of FWHM of intensity for τ_p
