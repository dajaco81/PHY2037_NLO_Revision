# L01 Digest — Welcome and Course Introduction

## Core Concepts

1. **The fundamental equation of NLO:**

$$\mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0\chi\mathbf{E}$$

Everything about how a material interacts with light is contained in the susceptibility tensor **χ**. The polarisation **P** (induced dipole moment per unit volume) is the response we measure or detect; **E** is the driving electric field.

2. **The nonlinear extension:**

$$\mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0[\chi^{(1)}\mathbf{E} + \chi^{(2)}\mathbf{E}^2 + \chi^{(3)}\mathbf{E}^3 + \cdots]$$

This is the foundation of nonlinear optics — higher-order terms allow polarisations at new frequencies.

3. **Rayleigh scattering** governs why the sky is blue: I_scattered ∝ 1/λ⁴ → short wavelengths scatter more. This recurring example motivates why NLO microscopy uses near-infrared excitation.

## Key Equations

| Equation | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **P** = ε₀χ**E** | Linear induced polarisation |
| **P** = ε₀[χ⁽¹⁾**E** + χ⁽²⁾**E**² + …] | Full nonlinear power series |
| I ∝ 1/λ⁴ | Rayleigh scattering intensity |

## Definitions

- **P (polarisation):** Induced dipole moment per unit volume. Units: C m⁻²
- **χ (susceptibility):** Material property that maps E onto P. Scalar for isotropic materials; tensor for anisotropic.
- **Contrast mechanism:** The physical process that makes different parts of an image look different
- **Spatial resolution:** Smallest separation between two points that can be distinguished as separate (not the same as magnification)

## Physical Intuition

Imagine applying an electric field to a material: the electrons get dragged slightly away from the positive nuclei → creates dipoles → each dipole emits its own EM radiation. The collective radiation from all dipoles is what we detect. The susceptibility χ encodes how easily this dragging happens, how it depends on frequency, and how the resulting radiation is generated.

The nonlinear extension: if you drive the electrons very hard (high electric field), they don't just move proportionally to E — they start to move in a nonlinear way, generating radiation at frequencies other than the driving frequency.

## History

- **1960:** First laser (Maiman) — ruby crystal + flash lamp
- **1961:** First SHG (Franken) — red laser → quartz crystal → tiny spot of blue light; signal so small the peer reviewer thought it was a photograph artifact
