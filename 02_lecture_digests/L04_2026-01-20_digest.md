# L04 Digest — Optical Imaging Part 2: Resolution, Confocal Microscopy, Scattering

## Core Concepts

1. **Numerical aperture** (NA = n·sinα) determines the light-gathering ability of an objective and sets the diffraction limit.

2. **Airy disk:** the diffraction pattern of a focused beam. 94% of light in central disk of radius r = 1.22λ/D.

3. **Abbe diffraction limit:** the minimum resolvable distance between two points ≈ λ/(2·NA). Resolution improves with shorter wavelength or higher NA.

4. **Rayleigh criterion:** two points just resolved when the maximum of one PSF coincides with the minimum of the other. The criterion converts from angular (1.22λ/D) to a distance via NA (see derivation).

5. **Gaussian PSF approximation:** the Airy disk's central lobe is approximated by a Gaussian — much easier to work with in calculations.

6. **Confocal principle:** pinholes at excitation and detection (both at the same focal distance) reject out-of-plane light → virtual optical sectioning of thick tissue.

7. **Galvanometer scanner:** two oscillating mirrors steer the laser in a raster pattern across the sample; the sample is stationary; enables 3D imaging.

8. **Rayleigh scattering (I ∝ 1/λ⁴) vs. depth:** doubling wavelength reduces scattering by 16×. Short wavelength needed for resolution; long wavelength needed for penetration — fundamental conflict.

9. **The fundamental tradeoff:** resolution (∝ λ), depth penetration (∝ 1/λ⁴), and contrast mechanism (dictates λ) cannot all be simultaneously optimised with linear optics.

## Key Equations

| Equation | Name |
|----------|------|
| NA = n·sinα | Numerical aperture |
| r = 1.22λ/D | Airy disk radius |
| d_lateral = λ/(2·NA) | Abbe lateral resolution limit |
| d_axial = 2λ/NA² | Abbe axial resolution limit |
| θ_min = 1.22λ/D → d = 0.61λ/NA | Rayleigh criterion (derived) |
| I_scatter ∝ 1/λ⁴ | Rayleigh scattering |

## Derivations

### Rayleigh Criterion → NA Formulation

> **[RED BOX — must be able to reproduce]**

Starting from the angular Rayleigh criterion: θ_min = 1.22λ/D

In a microscope, d = f·θ (small angle), and D = 2f·sinα (lens geometry) → D/f = 2sinα

$$d_{min} = \frac{1.22\lambda f}{D} = \frac{1.22\lambda}{2\sin\alpha} \approx \frac{0.61\lambda}{NA}$$

(The exact Abbe factor 0.5λ/NA and Rayleigh factor 0.61λ/NA differ slightly — know the derivation method, use the convention the lecturer specifies.)

## Definitions

- **PSF (Point Spread Function):** 3D intensity distribution at the focus of an objective; the "blurring function" of the imaging system
- **Confocal detection:** detection through a pinhole at the focal distance from the objective — rejects out-of-plane light
- **Numerical aperture:** NA = n·sinα; determines both spatial resolution and light collection efficiency
- **Galvanometer scanner:** fast-steering mirrors inside the microscope that raster the laser spot across the sample
- **Dichroic mirror:** reflects one wavelength range, transmits another — separates excitation from emission in a fluorescence microscope

## Physical Intuition

**Why confocal works:** The detection pinhole is placed exactly where the in-focus light converges. Out-of-focus light converges at a different point and hits the pinhole plane at the wrong position → physically blocked. Like using a very small hole to select only one depth.

**Why scattering defeats confocal:** Emitted photons from the correct focal plane scatter on the way back out and arrive at the pinhole at a wrong angle → rejected. The signal you want is lost; you can't recover it by increasing laser power without damaging the sample.

**Why IR is needed:** Reducing λ from 488 nm to 980 nm (×2 increase) → scattering reduced by 2⁴ = 16. But this doubles the diffraction limit. Nonlinear optics breaks this tradeoff.

## Common Mistakes

1. Confusing **resolution** with **magnification** (they are not the same)
2. Using the angular Rayleigh criterion (1.22λ/D) directly without converting to distance via NA
3. Forgetting that the confocal pinhole is **at the detection side** as well as the excitation side
4. Saying scattering only affects the excitation — it also removes signal photons that miss the detection pinhole
